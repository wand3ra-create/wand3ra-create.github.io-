Module 01

What Even Is a Robot?

Before you touch a single wire, you need to understand what you're actually building. And it's probably not what you think.

By the end of this module

  • Define what a robot actually is in simple terms
  • Identify the three things every robot does
  • Recognize robots in everyday objects around you

1.1Forget What Movies Told You

When most people hear the word robot, they picture something from a movie — metal legs, glowing eyes, maybe trying to take over the world. That picture is not useful to you here.

A robot doesn't have to look like a human. It doesn't have to be expensive. It doesn't have to be complicated. Here is the only definition you need:

A robot is anything that senses its environment, makes a decision based on what it sensed, and then acts.

1.2The Three Things Every Robot Does

Sense — A robot needs to know something about the world around it. It does this through sensors. A sensor converts something physical into an electrical signal.

Decide — Once the robot has information, something processes it. This is usually a small chip called a microcontroller. It runs code that says "if I sensed this, then do that."

Act — After deciding, the robot does something. The components that carry out actions are called actuators — motors, speakers, LEDs.

1.3You Already Own a Robot

Think about a phone you've used. Your phone is a robot. Not an exciting one, but technically — yes.

  • It senses — the accelerometer detects when you tilt it.
  • It decides — the processor runs code that says "screen tilted sideways, switch to landscape mode"
  • It acts — the screen rotates.

Try This — No Components Needed

Find three things around you that fit the Sense → Decide → Act pattern. A mousetrap counts. A door on a spring counts. If you can do this, you already think like an engineer.

Module 02

Electricity Is Just Water

You can't build anything without understanding electricity. The good news is you already understand it — you just don't know it yet.

By the end of this module

  • Understand voltage, current, and resistance using a simple analogy
  • Read a basic circuit diagram
  • Build your first circuit using salvaged components

2.1The Water Pipe Analogy

Electricity is invisible, which makes it feel complicated. But it behaves almost exactly like water flowing through pipes. Once you see this, it never goes away.

WaterElectricity
Water in the pipeElectrons in the wire
Water pressureVoltage (measured in Volts, V)
Flow rateCurrent (measured in Amperes, A)
A narrow pipeResistance (measured in Ohms, Ω)
A pumpA battery

High pressure pushes more water through. High voltage pushes more current through. A narrow pipe slows water down. High resistance slows current down. Same logic, different medium.

2.2Ohm's Law (Without the Fear)

There's one formula in basic electronics that connects all three concepts. It's called Ohm's Law:

Voltage = Current × Resistance  |  V = I × R

You don't need to memorise this right now. What matters is the relationship: if you increase resistance, current drops. If you increase voltage, current rises. The battery is the pump. The wire is the pipe. The component at the end is what the water powers.

2.3What Is a Circuit?

A circuit is just a closed loop that electricity can flow around. Break the loop anywhere — the flow stops. That's how a switch works. A switch is just a way to break and restore the loop on demand.

  • A power source (battery, USB, solar panel)
  • A conductor (wire, or any metal that lets electricity flow)
  • A load (the component doing something — a motor, an LED, a buzzer)
  • A return path (the negative wire going back to the battery)
Safety note: Never connect a battery directly to a wire with nothing in between. With no resistance, current spikes — the wire heats up fast, the battery drains instantly, and you can get burned. Always have a component in the loop.

Try This — Your First Circuit

Connect one wire from the positive end of the battery to one leg of the bulb. Connect another wire from the other leg of the bulb back to the negative end of the battery. If it lights up — congratulations. You just built a circuit.

Now touch the two wire ends together briefly before connecting the bulb. Notice how quickly the battery warms up with nothing resisting the current. That's why every circuit needs a load.
