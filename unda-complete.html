Module 01 — Foundation

What Even Is a Robot?

Before you touch a single wire, you need to actually understand what you're building. This one's all in your head — and that's the point.

~25 min read · No components needed

Terms Used in This Module

The words below will come up as you read through this module. Don't worry if some of them are new — each one is explained in context when it first appears. Skim through this list now, then come back to it any time something throws you off.

Sensor A component that detects something in the physical world — light, distance, temperature, touch, sound — and converts it into an electrical signal a machine can read.
Actuator A component that makes something happen in the physical world. Motors, speakers, and LEDs are all actuators — they turn electrical signals into visible, audible, or physical action.
LED Light Emitting Diode. A tiny component that produces light when electricity flows through it. The small indicator lights on phone chargers, Wi-Fi routers, and TV remotes are LEDs.
Microcontroller A tiny chip — smaller than your thumbnail — that acts as a robot's brain. It runs code you write and uses it to decide what the robot should do based on what its sensors are detecting.
Processor The part of a computer or phone that does all the thinking — it executes instructions at very high speed. Your device's processor handles everything from opening apps to running the camera.
GPU Graphics Processing Unit. A chip specialised in drawing images on a screen. When a phone rotates its display, the GPU redraws everything in the new orientation.
Accelerometer A sensor that measures movement and tilt. Built into almost every phone made in the last fifteen years — it's how your device knows which way it's facing and switches between portrait and landscape.
Infrared sensor A sensor that sends out invisible infrared light — the same type used in TV remotes — and detects when something breaks or reflects it back. Used in automatic taps, hand sanitisers, and obstacle detection.
Algorithm A set of step-by-step instructions for solving a problem. The "if this happens, do that" logic inside a robot's code is an algorithm. A recipe is an algorithm. Directions to a place are an algorithm.
Autonomous Able to make decisions on its own without a human controlling it in real-time. A self-driving car is autonomous. A remote-controlled car is not.
Hydraulic actuator A type of actuator that uses pressurised fluid instead of electricity alone to create powerful physical movement. Found in heavy construction machinery and in advanced robots that need serious force.
LIDAR Light Detection and Ranging. A sensor that fires rapid pulses of laser light in all directions and measures how long they take to bounce back — building a precise 3D map of the surroundings. Used in self-driving cars and advanced robots.
Stereo cameras Two cameras placed side by side, like human eyes. The slight difference in what each camera sees lets a robot calculate depth — how far away objects actually are.
IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit) A sensor that tracks orientation, rotation speed, and acceleration all at once. It tells a robot — or a drone, or a phone — exactly how it is tilted and how fast it's moving in any direction.
SDA Loop Sense → Decide → Act. The three steps every robot takes, repeated continuously as long as it is running. This is the central idea of the entire module.

  • Understand the three things every single robot on Earth does

1.1 Okay. But First — What Did You Come Here For?

Be honest. You didn't show up here because you wanted to learn about resistors or circuit diagrams. You're here because somewhere in the back of your mind you've got an image. Maybe it's Iron Man's suit snapping together piece by piece around him before he flies off. Maybe it's Optimus Prime mid-transformation on a highway. Maybe it's the scene in I, Robot where thousands of machines move in perfect coordination, and something in you thought — yeah. I want to understand how that works. I want to build something like that.

Good. That's the right reason to be here.

But here's the thing nobody says at the start: those robots are not magic. Iron Man's suit is not science fiction in the sense of being impossible — the principles behind it are completely real. The gap between what Tony Stark builds in those films and what you'll build by Module 8 is not a gap in fundamental ideas. It's a gap in budget, materials, and years of engineering iteration. The underlying ideas are the same. You're about to learn those ideas.

So yes — Iron Man, the Terminator, the droids from Star Wars — we'll come back to all of them throughout this module. But first, let's settle what a robot actually is. Because the answer will probably surprise you.

1.2 Forget Everything Movies Told You

When most people hear "robot," they picture one of two things. Either a humanoid machine with glowing red eyes coming for someone's job (or someone's life, depending on which film they watched last), or a small, lovable beeping thing like R2-D2 that somehow fixes spaceships without having proper hands.

Both of those are robots. But so is this:

  • The automatic hand sanitiser dispenser at a hospital entrance
  • A modern washing machine that adjusts its cycle based on how heavy the load is
  • A drone delivering a package to a rooftop
  • A thermostat that switches on a fan when the room temperature exceeds a set level
  • A traffic light that detects cars waiting and adjusts its own timing

None of those look like the Terminator. But every single one is a robot — because they all do the same three things. And those three things are the entire foundation of robotics as a field.

A robot is anything that senses its environment, processes that information to make a decision, and then acts on that decision. That's the whole definition. Everything else is just details.

The reason robotics feels complicated is not because the core idea is hard — it's because each of those three steps can become very, very deep. But the idea itself? Three steps. You just read it.

1.3 The Three Things Every Robot Does

Let's go through those three steps carefully, because they are the skeleton of everything in this course. Every module, every circuit, every line of code you write will serve one of these three things.

01

Sense

A robot needs information about the world around it. Without that, it can't do anything useful — it's operating blind. The components that gather this information are called sensors, and a sensor does one specific job: it converts something physical into an electrical signal the robot can read.

Temperature, distance, pressure, light, sound, motion — sensors exist for all of these. Your eyes are sensors. Your skin is a sensor. The microphone on any device is a sensor. Even the sharp pain you feel when you step on something in the dark — your nervous system just ran a sensing operation and screamed the result at your brain.

02

Decide

Once the robot has information, something has to process it and choose what to do next. This is the brain. In the robots you'll build in this course, that brain is a microcontroller — a chip roughly the size of your thumbnail that runs whatever code you write for it.

The decision-making is pure logic. "If the distance sensor reads less than 10cm, stop moving forward." That's a decision. It can get far more complex than that — a self-driving car's algorithms are making thousands of decisions per second — but every single one is built from the same basic pattern: input arrives, a condition gets checked, an action gets selected.

This is why writing code is central to robotics. The microcontroller does not think on its own. It executes exactly what you tell it to, nothing more. You are the intelligence behind the machine. Code is how you put that intelligence inside it.

03

Act

After deciding, the robot does something in the physical world. The components that carry out actions are actuators. Motors spin things. Speakers produce sound. LEDs — those tiny components that produce light when electricity flows through them, the ones blinking on your charger and router right now — light up on command. All of these take an electrical signal and turn it into something physical: movement, sound, light.

This is the step people find most satisfying, because you can see it. Something moves. Something glows. But notice — it's the last step. You can't have a good action without solid sensing and good decision-making underneath it. The act is only as good as the thinking that preceded it.

1.4 Let's Put It Together: The SDA Loop

Sense → Decide → Act. It's called the SDA loop — and it's a loop because after the robot acts, it immediately senses again to check the result of what it just did, and the whole thing repeats. Continuously. As long as the robot is running.

You run SDA loops constantly without realising it. Right now, your eyes are sensing the text on this screen, your brain is deciding what the words mean, and your eyes are acting by moving to the next line. Loop complete. Next one already started.

SDA Loop — Worked Example: Automatic Hand Sanitiser

Sense: An infrared sensor — a component that fires an invisible beam of light and waits for it to bounce back, the same technology used in TV remotes — sends out that beam continuously. When your hand enters the range and the reflection changes, the sensor sends a signal: something is here.

Decide: The microcontroller receives that signal. Its code says: "if object detected within range, trigger the pump." One condition. One instruction.

Act: A small motor activates. The pump pushes sanitiser out.

The components in that dispenser cost very little. The concept powering it is identical to the concept inside a surgical robot worth millions. Scale and precision differ. The loop does not.

Try This — No Components Needed

Hunt for SDA Loops

Find three things near you right now — in your room, your compound, or outside — that follow the Sense → Decide → Act pattern. Write down what each step is for each one.

To get you started:

  • A mousetrap — senses pressure on the trigger plate, "decides" mechanically to release the spring, acts by snapping shut
  • A hydraulic door closer (the metal arm at the top of heavy doors) — senses via spring tension that the door is open, acts by pulling it slowly and smoothly shut
  • Screen timeout on any device — senses no touch input for a set number of seconds, decides the screen should switch off, acts by locking and dimming

If you can do this, you are already thinking like a roboticist. That is not an exaggeration.

1.5 Whatever Device You're Using Right Now Is a Robot

Phone, laptop, tablet, desktop — whatever you're reading this on, it is a robot. Not a dramatic one. But technically, completely, yes.

Take screen rotation. When you tilt a phone sideways, the display switches from tall-and-narrow to wide-and-short. That feels trivial. It's a full SDA loop running in under a second.

SDA Step What Happens The Component Doing It
Sense The device detects it has been tilted sideways Accelerometer — a sensor that measures tilt and movement, built into almost every phone made in the last fifteen years
Decide The processor checks: "Is screen rotation enabled? Is the tilt past the threshold angle?" If both yes — rotate. The processor running the operating system (Android, iOS, Windows — whichever the device runs)
Act The display switches from portrait to landscape orientation The GPU — the chip responsible for drawing images on screen — redraws everything in the new layout

Do the same analysis with camera auto-focus — senses image blur, decides which direction to shift the lens, the lens motor acts. With fingerprint unlock — senses the ridge pattern, decides if it matches, acts by unlocking or refusing. With the function on some smartwatches that detects a fall and calls for help automatically. Every single one: SDA.

Your device is running dozens of these loops simultaneously all the time. It's not one robot — it's many robots sharing the same hardware, stacked on top of each other inside one rectangle of glass and metal.

JARVIS — the AI assistant Tony Stark builds to run his suit, his lab, and his house in the Iron Man films — is essentially a device. An extraordinarily powerful, ambient, voice-activated one. He senses through cameras, microphones, and network feeds. He decides using AI. He acts by controlling everything around him. Tony Stark engineered the world's most capable device and gave it a British accent and a name. The principle is identical to the device you're holding or sitting in front of right now.

1.6 The Two Kinds of Robots

One more distinction before we go further — this one will save you a lot of confusion later. Robots fall into two broad categories based on how they handle decisions:

Type How it decides Real-world examples
Pre-programmed Follows fixed rules only. If X happens, always do Y. No flexibility, no adaptation to new situations. Automatic hand sanitiser, traffic lights on a fixed timer, a factory arm that welds the same joint ten thousand times a day
Autonomous Gathers data and figures out what to do even in situations it hasn't encountered before. Can adapt when things change unexpectedly. Self-driving cars, Mars rovers, modern warehouse robots that reroute around obstacles

The T-800 — the Terminator from the 1984 film — is an autonomous robot. It gathers information about its target, adapts its approach when blocked, and changes its plan dynamically based on what it's observing. That matches the definition exactly. The bomb disposal robots used by military units across the world today are mostly pre-programmed and remote-controlled. One is a Hollywood creation. The other has saved actual lives. Both are robots. Neither is more legitimate than the other.

The robots you'll build in this course start in the first category and move toward the second. Module 5 is where that transition begins — when we introduce code that responds dynamically to live sensor input instead of just executing a fixed script.

Extra — If You're Curious Why humanoid robots are hard to build: Robots that walk on two legs and manipulate objects with fingers are among the most difficult to engineer — not because the SDA loop is different for them, but because keeping a two-legged machine upright on uneven ground requires the loop to run hundreds of times per second with extremely precise sensors and actuators all working together. Boston Dynamics' robot Atlas — if you've seen it doing backflips online — uses over 28 hydraulic actuators (powerful fluid-driven joints, the kind you find in heavy construction equipment, miniaturised for a robot body), combined with LIDAR, stereo cameras, and an IMU just to maintain balance while moving. A toddler learning to walk is solving the exact same balance problem, unconsciously, in real-time. Toddlers are genuinely underrated engineers.

Electricity, What You Actually Need to Know

This wasn't fun in school. It probably won't be thrilling here either. But it is necessary, and once you understand it, everything else in this course will actually make sense.

A note on scope. Electrical engineering is an entire university degree. This module is not that. What you'll get here is the minimum foundation you need to understand the circuits in this course: voltage, current, resistance, and how they relate. As you go further (in later modules and eventually beyond this course), you'll find yourself coming back to electricity, going a layer deeper each time. That depth will make sense when you have the context to hold it. For now, we are building the floor.

Terms Used in This Module

Skim these before you start. Come back whenever something in the lesson throws you off.

Electron A tiny particle that exists inside every atom. Electrons carry a negative electrical charge. When electrons move through a material in the same direction, that movement is what we call electric current.
Electric current The flow of electrons through a conductor (like a wire). Measured in amperes, usually shortened to amps (A). Think of it as how much electricity is actually moving.
Voltage The force that pushes electrons through a circuit. Measured in volts (V). A higher voltage pushes harder. A AA battery is 1.5V. A phone charger is usually 5V. A wall socket is 220–240V depending on where you are.

Circuit A closed loop through which electricity flows. If the loop is broken anywhere — a wire disconnected, a switch open — the current stops. Every electronic device is a circuit.
Conductor A material that allows electrons to flow through it easily. Copper wire is a conductor. Most metals are conductors. This is why wires are made of metal.
Insulator A material that blocks electron flow. Rubber and plastic are insulators. This is why wires are coated in plastic — to stop the electricity from going somewhere it shouldn't.
Resistor A small component specifically designed to reduce current in a circuit. Used to protect sensitive components from receiving too much electricity.

Ground (GND) The reference point in a circuit — the "zero" that all other voltages are measured from. Every circuit needs a ground. On most circuit boards it's labelled GND.

Alternating current (AC) Electricity that rapidly switches direction — back and forth — many times per second. What comes out of wall sockets. We will not be working with AC in this course. Wall sockets can kill you. We are using batteries.
Power (Watts) How much energy a component is using or producing per second. Calculated as Voltage × Current. A phone charger rated at 10W delivers 10 joules of energy per second to your battery.

2.1 Why Electricity Matters for Robotics

Look, I know. Electricity was not the highlight of physics class. It's invisible, the formulas feel disconnected from anything real, and the teacher probably drew a lot of arrows on the board without explaining why any of it mattered. That's a fair experience to have had. But here's the thing: in robotics, it matters immediately and concretely. Everything in your robot runs on it. The sensors, the microcontroller, the motors (all of it) are powered by and controlled through electrical signals. You can't get around this one.

The good news is that the electricity we use in this course is not complicated or dangerous. We work with batteries, small low-voltage power sources. The kind of electricity that comes out of a wall socket is a completely different situation, and we will not be touching it. Not here, not without proper training.

Every single thing your robot does (sensing, deciding, acting) is ultimately an electrical signal moving through a circuit. Understand the circuit, and you understand the machine.

Most people get intimidated by electricity before they even start, because it tends to get introduced with too many equations and too little intuition. So we're doing it the other way. Intuition first, then the equation. By the end of this module, Ohm's Law will feel obvious rather than arbitrary, and you'll understand why it's the one formula worth memorising.

2.2 What Electricity Actually Is

Everything around you is made of atoms. Inside every atom, there are tiny particles called electrons, and electrons carry electrical charge. In most materials, electrons stay roughly in place (they don't go anywhere). But in certain materials, particularly metals like copper, electrons are loosely held and can be made to move.

When a huge number of electrons all move in the same direction through a material, that movement is electric current. That's it. Electricity is literally just electrons moving. The reason it can do useful things (power a light, spin a motor, run a processor) is that moving electrons carry energy, and that energy can be converted into other forms.

You don't need to think about individual electrons when building circuits. What you need is an understanding of the three properties that describe how that movement behaves: voltage, current, and resistance. Everything else in this module comes from those three.

2.3 The Three Properties: Voltage, Current, Resistance

V

Voltage — the push

Voltage is the force that drives electrons through a circuit. Measured in volts (V), it's essentially electrical pressure. The higher the voltage, the harder electrons are being pushed.

A standard AA battery gives you 1.5V. A USB phone charger outputs 5V. The microcontrollers used in robotics typically run on 3.3V or 5V. A wall socket in most of Africa carries 220–240V. Same concept, very different magnitudes, and very different consequences if something goes wrong at each level.

One thing that trips people up early: voltage is always measured between two points, not at a single point. A 5V power supply doesn't mean "5 volts exists here." It means there's a 5V difference between the positive terminal and the negative terminal (ground). That difference is what pushes current through your circuit.

I

Current — the flow

Current is how much charge is actually moving through the circuit per second. Measured in amperes (amps, or A), or milliamps (mA) for smaller amounts. 1000mA = 1A.

Voltage causes current. The push produces the flow. But how much current flows for a given voltage depends on the third property: resistance.

Current matters because every component has a limit. An LED typically survives on about 20mA. Give it significantly more than that without protection and it burns out in under a second. This is not a hypothetical. You will burn out LEDs in this course. Almost everyone does at least once, and that's fine. Cheap lesson.

R

Resistance — the opposition

Resistance is how much a material opposes the flow of electrons. Measured in ohms (Ω, the Greek letter omega). Every component and wire in a circuit has some resistance, and high resistance means less current gets through for the same voltage.

This is actually useful. Resistors (components designed specifically to have a known, stable resistance) are how we control how much current reaches sensitive components. They're among the most common components in any circuit, they cost almost nothing, and their entire job is to limit current so things don't blow up.

2.4 Ohm's Law: The One Equation You Need

Voltage, current, and resistance are not independent of each other. They are locked together in a relationship called Ohm's Law:

V
Voltage (Volts)
I
Current (Amps)
R
Resistance (Ohms)
V = I × R Voltage = Current × Resistance
I = V ÷ R Current = Voltage ÷ Resistance
R = V ÷ I Resistance = Voltage ÷ Current

Same equation, rearranged three ways depending on what you need to find. In practice, the version you'll use most is the second one (calculating current), because the question you'll ask most often when building a circuit is: "how much current is actually flowing through this component right now?"

Worked Example: LED + Resistor

You have a 5V power supply and an LED. The LED needs to run at around 20mA (0.02A) to be bright without burning out. How much resistance do you need?

R = V ÷ I

R = 5 ÷ 0.02

R = 250 ohms

So you'd use a 250Ω resistor (or the nearest standard value above it, which is typically 270Ω or 330Ω — rounding up is always safer than rounding down). Without that resistor, the LED sees the full 5V with almost no resistance, current surges, and the LED dies in under a second.

This calculation — or a variation of it — will come up every time you add an LED to a circuit. Once you've done it a few times, it becomes automatic.

2.5 What a Circuit Actually Is

A circuit is a closed loop. Electricity flows from the positive terminal of a power source, through the components, and back to the negative terminal. If that loop is broken anywhere (a wire comes loose, a switch opens, a component fails) current stops completely. The whole thing goes dead.

This is not a technicality. It is the most fundamental rule in electronics. An open loop is not a slow circuit or a weak circuit. It's no circuit at all. When something in your build stops working and you can't figure out why, nine times out of ten it's a broken loop somewhere: a loose connection, a wire not seated fully, a component in backwards. Finding the break is usually the entire job of troubleshooting.

A basic circuit: 5V battery → 330Ω resistor → LED → back to battery. A complete loop.

Power leaves the battery's positive terminal, passes through a resistor (limiting the current), then through an LED (converting electrical energy into light), and returns to the negative terminal. Take out any one connection and nothing works. That's every circuit, from the simplest LED loop to the processor running your device, at its core.

2.6 Reading a Circuit Diagram

A circuit diagram (also called a schematic) is a map of a circuit drawn using standard symbols instead of pictures of real components. Engineers use them because they're faster to draw, easier to read, and universal. A schematic drawn in Kenya means exactly the same thing to an engineer in Brazil or South Korea. Once you can read them, you can follow any electronics tutorial in the world regardless of where it was made.

You don't need to memorise every symbol right now. These are the ones that appear in this course:

Component Symbol Description What It Does
Battery / Power supply Long line (+) next to short line (−), repeated Provides the voltage that drives the circuit
Resistor A rectangle or a zigzag line Limits current flow to protect components
LED A triangle pointing into a vertical line, with two arrows indicating light Emits light when current passes through it in the correct direction
Switch A line with a gap and a hinged segment that can close it Opens or closes the circuit loop — controls whether current flows
Ground (GND) A horizontal line with progressively shorter lines below it, like a downward triangle The reference point — the circuit's zero voltage level
Wire connection A filled dot where two lines meet Confirms the wires are actually connected (not just crossing)

One thing worth remembering specifically: when lines cross in a diagram without a dot, those wires are not connected. They just happen to cross visually. The dot is what makes a connection. This catches a lot of beginners out, including people who've been building circuits for years.

2.7 Series vs Parallel: Two Ways to Connect Things

When you have more than one component in a circuit, there are two fundamental ways to connect them. They behave completely differently and understanding which is which will save you a lot of confusion later.

Series — one after another

Components connected in series sit in a single line. Current has to pass through all of them one by one. There's only one path for electricity to take.

Consequence: If one component in a series circuit fails or is disconnected, the entire circuit stops. The current has nowhere to go. This is why old-style Christmas lights would all go out when one bulb died — they were wired in series.

Voltage in series: The voltage gets shared across all components. Two LEDs in series with a 5V supply each see roughly 2.5V instead of 5V.

Parallel — side by side

Components connected in parallel each get their own path from the power source. Current splits and some goes through each branch.

Consequence: If one component fails, the rest continue working — electricity simply flows through the other branches. Modern buildings and vehicles wire their lights in parallel for exactly this reason.

Voltage in parallel: Every component connected in parallel sees the same voltage. Two LEDs in parallel both see the full 5V.

Most real circuits use a combination of both. The circuits you'll build from Module 5 onward will have components in both configurations. When something isn't behaving the way you expect, series vs. parallel is one of the first things worth checking.

2.8 What Happens When Things Go Wrong

Two failure modes are worth understanding before you start building. Both happen to beginners, and knowing them in advance saves components (and occasionally prevents a battery from getting very hot very fast).

Short circuit. This happens when electricity finds a path of almost no resistance — skipping the components it was supposed to go through and flowing directly between positive and negative. With resistance near zero, Ohm's Law tells you current goes extremely high (I = V ÷ nearly 0 = very large number). That surge of current generates intense heat almost instantly. Batteries can rupture. Components can burn. Wires can melt. A short circuit in a low-voltage battery circuit is unlikely to seriously hurt you, but it will destroy your components and possibly your battery. Always double-check your wiring before connecting power.
Exceeding a component's current rating. Every component has a maximum current it can handle. Exceed it — even briefly — and the component fails. LEDs blow. Microcontroller pins fry. Motors overheat. The fix is always Ohm's Law: calculate the current before you connect anything, and add a resistor if the number is too high.

Neither of these is catastrophic at the voltages we're working with. A destroyed LED costs almost nothing. But the habit of checking your circuit before powering it is worth building from day one, because that same habit applied to higher-voltage work later is what keeps engineers safe.

2.9 DC vs AC — Know the Difference, Respect the Difference

Direct current (DC) flows in one direction only. Batteries produce it. Solar panels produce it. All the circuits in this course run on DC, specifically the low-voltage kind that comes out of batteries or USB power banks. Safe to work with at the voltages we use.

Alternating current (AC) rapidly flips direction, back and forth, typically 50 or 60 times per second depending on your country. It's what comes out of wall sockets. AC is used for power distribution because it transmits more efficiently over long distances, which is why the national grid runs on it. It is also significantly more dangerous than DC at the same voltage, and wall sockets in most of Africa carry 220–240V, which is more than enough to kill someone.

We do not work with AC in this course. Everything we build is powered by batteries or USB power banks, low-voltage DC only. If you ever find yourself thinking about plugging a circuit you've built directly into a wall socket, stop. That is a completely different domain of engineering with a completely different set of safety requirements. Come back to it when you've had proper training.

The reason this comes up now is that as you progress (other tutorials, YouTube projects, more advanced builds) you'll inevitably encounter AC circuits. You need to know immediately and without confusion that what you see there is different from what you're doing here.

Terms Used in This Module

Skim through these before you start. Come back whenever something throws you off mid-lesson.

Breadboard A reusable board with a grid of holes connected internally by metal strips. Used to build and test circuits without soldering anything permanently. You push components and wires into the holes and the connections form automatically.
Soldering The process of permanently joining two metal surfaces by melting a soft metal alloy (solder) between them and letting it cool. Creates a reliable electrical and mechanical connection. The opposite of breadboarding.
Pin A metal leg on a component or circuit board. Pins are how components connect to circuits — you either insert them into a breadboard, solder them to a PCB, or connect wires to them.
PCB (Printed Circuit Board) A flat board made of fibreglass with copper tracks printed onto it that form the wiring of a circuit. Almost every piece of electronics you own has at least one PCB inside it.
PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) A technique for controlling the effective power delivered to a component by rapidly switching voltage on and off. Used to control motor speed and LED brightness without wasting energy as heat.
Analog signal A signal that can be any value within a range — like a volume dial that goes from 0 to 10 with every value in between available. Most sensors produce analog signals.
Digital signal A signal that is either fully on or fully off — no in-between. Either 1 or 0. High or low. Most microcontrollers communicate using digital signals.
I2C / SPI / UART Communication protocols — standardised rules for how components talk to each other digitally. You don't need to understand them deeply right now; just know they exist and that different components use different ones.
Polarity The orientation of a component relative to positive and negative. Some components (LEDs, capacitors, motors) only work correctly when connected the right way around. Connect them backwards and they either don't work or get damaged.
Capacitance The ability to store electrical charge. Measured in farads (F), though most capacitors used in robotics are rated in microfarads (µF) or picofarads (pF).
Inductance The property of a component that opposes changes in current. Measured in henries (H). Relevant mostly in motors and wireless power circuits.
Logic level The voltage that a microcontroller uses to represent digital HIGH (1) and LOW (0). Most common logic levels are 3.3V and 5V. Mixing components with different logic levels without proper conversion can damage them.

Resistor
Passive · Current Control

You met resistors in Module 02. They do one thing: resist the flow of current. That resistance is measured in ohms (Ω). The more resistance, the less current gets through for a given voltage.

Physically, a standard resistor is a small cylinder with coloured bands painted on it and a metal leg (called a lead or pin) coming out of each end. The coloured bands are a code that tells you the resistance value without needing to measure it. There are four-band and five-band resistors; four-band is more common in beginner kits. You'll learn to read the colour code — it takes about ten minutes and you'll never forget it.

Resistors have no polarity. You can put them in either way around and they work the same. They're also extremely robust. The main way to kill a resistor is to run more power through it than its wattage rating allows, at which point it gets hot, smells terrible, and eventually fails. In the circuits you'll build in this course, that's unlikely to happen.

Where you'll use them: Everywhere. Protecting LEDs, setting sensor sensitivity, creating voltage dividers (a way to reduce voltage to a lower level), pulling signal lines to a known state. Resistors are the most common component in any circuit. Buy a kit of assorted values — you'll use all of them eventually.

LED
Active · Light Output · Actuator

Light Emitting Diode. A component that produces light when current flows through it in the correct direction. LEDs come in every colour (red, green, blue, white, yellow, infrared, ultraviolet) and in a wide range of sizes and brightness levels.

Physically, a standard through-hole LED looks like a small dome of coloured plastic with two metal legs. The longer leg is the anode (positive, where current enters) and the shorter leg is the cathode (negative, where current exits). There's also usually a flat edge on the base of the dome on the cathode side. Polarity matters — put it in backwards and it simply won't light up. No damage, it just won't work.

LEDs have a forward voltage (the minimum voltage needed to turn them on) and a maximum current rating. Exceed the current rating and they burn out, usually instantly and permanently. A current-limiting resistor in series with every LED is not optional — it's mandatory every single time.

Beyond visible light LEDs, there are infrared LEDs (used in TV remotes and obstacle sensors), UV LEDs, and RGB LEDs (which contain red, green, and blue elements in one package and can produce any colour by mixing them).

Capacitor
Passive · Energy Storage · Signal Filtering

A capacitor stores electrical charge temporarily and releases it when needed. Think of it as a very small, fast-charging battery — it can't hold nearly as much energy as a battery, but it can charge and discharge much faster.

There are two main types you'll encounter. Electrolytic capacitors are cylindrical, usually blue or black, with two legs of different lengths (longer = positive). They have polarity and must be connected the right way around — reverse them and they can fail, sometimes violently. They handle larger capacitance values. Ceramic capacitors are small, flat, and disc-shaped. They have no polarity, are more stable, and handle smaller capacitance values.

In robotics, capacitors do a few important jobs. They smooth out voltage fluctuations (when a motor suddenly draws a lot of current, the voltage can dip — a capacitor across the power supply absorbs that dip). They filter noise from sensor signals. And they act as decoupling capacitors, placed near microcontrollers to keep their power supply stable.

You'll often see a 100nF ceramic capacitor placed right next to a microcontroller's power pin in circuit diagrams. That's a decoupling capacitor doing its job quietly in the background.

Transistor
Active · Switching · Amplification

The transistor is arguably the most important invention of the 20th century. Every processor, every microcontroller, every piece of modern electronics exists because of the transistor. A modern smartphone processor contains over 15 billion of them. They're about 7 nanometres across. You cannot see them with your eyes or even with most microscopes.

But the transistors you'll work with are much larger and do a simpler, more visible job: they act as electronically controlled switches. A small current or voltage applied to one pin (the base or gate) controls whether a much larger current can flow between the other two pins (collector/emitter or drain/source).

The two main families you'll encounter are BJTs (Bipolar Junction Transistors, controlled by current) and MOSFETs (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistors, controlled by voltage). For robotics beginners, MOSFETs are increasingly the default choice because they're efficient and easy to control with a microcontroller.

In practical robotics, transistors let a microcontroller (which can only output a small amount of current) control components that need much more power, like motors. The microcontroller signal goes to the transistor's control pin; the transistor handles the heavy current itself.

Diode
Passive · One-Way Current Flow · Protection

A diode allows current to flow in only one direction. That's its entire function. Current can go from anode to cathode (forward direction) but not the other way. In the forward direction, there's a small voltage drop across the diode (typically 0.6–0.7V for silicon diodes). In reverse, it blocks almost completely.

Physically, a standard diode looks similar to a resistor — a small cylinder with legs — but has a stripe marking the cathode end. Always check the marking before connecting.

In robotics, diodes serve several important protective roles. A flyback diode (also called a freewheeling diode) is placed across a motor or any inductive load — when the motor is switched off, it generates a brief voltage spike in the reverse direction (a property of inductors) that can destroy a transistor or microcontroller pin. The flyback diode gives that spike a safe path to flow through instead.

Schottky diodes are a faster, lower-voltage-drop variant used in power circuits where efficiency matters. Zener diodes are a special type that allow current to flow backwards above a specific voltage, used to create stable voltage references.

Microcontroller
Active · The Brain · Decide

The microcontroller is the brain of the robot. It's a chip that contains a processor, memory, and input/output pins all in one package. You write code, load it onto the microcontroller, and it executes that code to read sensors, make decisions, and control actuators.

The most common microcontroller platform for beginners is the Arduino — specifically the Arduino Uno, which is built around the ATmega328P chip. It has 14 digital pins (can read or output HIGH/LOW signals), 6 analog input pins (can read voltage levels), a USB connection for programming, and runs on 5V. It can be powered by USB, a 9V battery with a barrel jack, or 5V directly to its power pin.

The Arduino Nano is a smaller version of the Uno with the same capabilities in a much smaller footprint — useful for builds where space is tight. The ESP8266 and ESP32 are more powerful microcontrollers with built-in Wi-Fi (and Bluetooth on the ESP32), making them ideal for IoT (Internet of Things) projects. The Raspberry Pi Pico, based on the RP2040 chip, is a newer and very capable option that can be programmed in Python.

In this course, we use the Arduino Uno as the default because it's the most documented, most supported, and easiest to debug when things go wrong. Most concepts transfer directly to other platforms once you understand them on the Uno.

DC Motor
Active · Rotation · Actuator

A DC motor converts electrical energy into rotational mechanical energy. Apply voltage across its two terminals and the shaft spins. Reverse the polarity (swap positive and negative) and the shaft spins the other direction. Simple, reliable, and the backbone of most wheeled robots.

The speed of a DC motor is roughly proportional to the voltage applied — higher voltage means faster spinning. The torque (turning force) is proportional to the current. A motor stalling (trying to turn but being physically stopped) draws very high current, which can damage the motor driver or the motor itself if sustained.

Standard small DC motors (the kind used in toy cars and beginner robots) cannot be connected directly to a microcontroller pin. The current they draw far exceeds what a microcontroller pin can provide. They need a motor driver — a dedicated chip that handles the current while the microcontroller just sends control signals.

Most small DC motors also come with a gearbox — a set of gears that reduces the speed and increases the torque. A motor spinning at 10,000 RPM through a 100:1 gearbox gives you 100 RPM with 100 times the torque. This trade-off between speed and torque is fundamental to motor selection for any project.

Servo Motor
Active · Precise Rotation · Actuator

A servo motor is a DC motor combined with a gearbox, a position sensor, and a control circuit, all in one small package. Unlike a DC motor (which just spins continuously), a servo motor moves to a specific angle and holds it there. Tell it to go to 90 degrees and it goes to 90 degrees. Tell it to go to 45 degrees and it goes to 45 degrees.

Standard hobby servos rotate between 0 and 180 degrees. Continuous rotation servos (a modified version) spin freely but allow you to control speed and direction instead of position — they behave more like a regular DC motor but with simpler control.

Servos are controlled using PWM signals (Pulse Width Modulation — covered in the glossary). The Arduino has a built-in Servo library that handles the PWM timing for you, so controlling a servo is as simple as writing servo.write(90) in your code.

They have three wires: power (usually red, connected to 5V), ground (usually brown or black), and signal (usually orange or yellow, connected to a PWM-capable Arduino pin). That signal wire is what carries the position command.

You'll find servos in robotic arms, camera gimbals (systems that keep a camera stable and level), steering mechanisms in RC cars, and anywhere that precise angular positioning is needed.

Motor Driver (H-Bridge)
Active · Motor Control · Interface

A motor driver is the component that sits between your microcontroller and your DC motor. The microcontroller cannot supply enough current to drive a motor directly — its pins would burn out. The motor driver accepts low-current control signals from the microcontroller and uses them to control a much larger current flowing to the motor from the main power supply.

The most common configuration inside a motor driver is called an H-bridge — a circuit of four switches (usually transistors or MOSFETs) arranged so that by opening and closing different combinations, you can send current through the motor in either direction. This is what lets you reverse a DC motor from software without rewiring anything.

The L298N is the most common beginner motor driver module. It comes on a red PCB with heat sinks (small metal fins that dissipate heat), and it can drive two DC motors simultaneously. It handles up to 2A per channel. The L293D is an older, lower-current option (600mA per channel) that comes in an IC chip package instead of a module.

For higher-power motors, the BTS7960 and DRV8833 are popular choices. The DRV8833 is particularly interesting because it's compact, efficient, and can drive motors from 3.3V logic — making it compatible with both Arduino and ESP32-based systems.

Ultrasonic Distance Sensor
Active · Distance Sensing · Sensor

The ultrasonic sensor (most commonly the HC-SR04) measures distance by sending out a pulse of ultrasonic sound (above the range of human hearing) and measuring how long it takes to bounce back off an object. The microcontroller then calculates distance from that time using the speed of sound.

The HC-SR04 has four pins: VCC (5V power), GND, Trig (trigger — you send a short pulse here to initiate a measurement), and Echo (the sensor holds this pin HIGH for as long as the sound pulse is travelling). Distance in centimetres = (time in microseconds) ÷ 58. The Arduino handles all of this timing.

Range is typically 2cm to 400cm with about 3mm accuracy. It doesn't work well on soft, sound-absorbing surfaces (like fabric or foam) or at very steep angles, because the sound doesn't bounce back cleanly. It also has a blind zone directly in front of it at distances under 2cm.

This is the sensor you'll use for obstacle avoidance in the Module 7 rover build. Point it forward, measure the distance to whatever is ahead, and if the reading drops below a threshold, the robot stops or turns.

Infrared (IR) Sensor
Active · Proximity / Line Detection · Sensor

An infrared sensor emits IR light (invisible to human eyes) and detects how much of it bounces back from nearby surfaces. The amount of reflection tells it whether something is close (more reflection) or far/absent (less reflection). The same principle is in every TV remote control you've ever used — the remote sends coded IR pulses, the TV's IR receiver reads them.

In robotics, IR sensors come in two main forms. Proximity IR sensors detect whether an object is within a short range (typically 2–30cm). Line-following IR sensors detect the difference in reflectivity between a dark line on a light surface (or vice versa) — black absorbs IR, white reflects it. This is the foundation of line-following robots, a classic robotics project that uses multiple IR sensors placed under the robot to keep it following a drawn track.

The TCRT5000 is a very common line-following/proximity IR sensor module. The Sharp GP2Y0A21 is a more accurate analog distance sensor that uses IR and can give a voltage output proportional to distance.

Temperature & Humidity Sensor
Active · Environmental Sensing · Sensor

The DHT11 and DHT22 are the two most common temperature and humidity sensors in beginner robotics. They measure both temperature and relative humidity and communicate with the microcontroller over a single data wire using a simple digital protocol.

The DHT11 is cheaper and less accurate (temperature: ±2°C, humidity: ±5%). The DHT22 is more expensive but significantly more accurate (temperature: ±0.5°C, humidity: ±2–5%) and covers a wider range. For most beginner projects, the DHT11 is fine.

These sensors matter for robotics in agricultural applications (monitoring crop conditions), environmental monitoring systems, and any automation system that needs to respond to temperature or humidity. Given that a large number of people building robots in sub-Saharan Africa are working on problems that touch agriculture and environment, these sensors are worth knowing well.

Reading them in code is simple — there's a widely used Arduino library (the DHT library by Adafruit) that handles all the timing and data parsing. A single function call returns the temperature and humidity as numbers you can work with directly.

Soil Moisture Sensor
Active · Environmental Sensing · Sensor

A soil moisture sensor measures how wet the soil is by measuring the electrical resistance (or capacitance, in better models) of the soil between two probes. Wet soil conducts electricity better than dry soil, so the resistance drops as moisture increases. The microcontroller reads this as a voltage on an analog pin.

There are two main types. Resistive sensors (the cheap kind, with two bare metal probes) work but corrode quickly because passing current through soil causes electrolysis that degrades the probes. They're fine for testing but not for permanent installations. Capacitive sensors (typically yellow PCBs) measure capacitance rather than resistance and don't corrode — much better for anything meant to run for weeks or months.

The simplest agricultural automation project you can build with an Arduino is a plant watering system: a soil moisture sensor triggers a small pump when the soil gets too dry. That's a complete, useful, real-world robot using the exact SDA loop from Module 1.

LDR (Light Dependent Resistor)
Passive · Light Sensing · Sensor

An LDR (also called a photoresistor) changes its resistance based on how much light hits it. In bright light, resistance is low (typically 1–10kΩ). In darkness, resistance is high (can reach 1MΩ or more). Pair it with a fixed resistor to form a voltage divider and you get a voltage that changes with light level — readable by an analog pin on an Arduino.

LDRs are cheap, widely available, and have no polarity. They're slow (they respond in milliseconds, not microseconds) which makes them useless for detecting fast-moving light patterns but perfect for detecting ambient light levels: is it day or night? Is the room lit or dark?

Common applications include automatic street lights (turn on when it gets dark), light-following robots (two LDRs on opposite sides — the robot steers toward whichever side has more light), and solar tracking systems (keeping a panel pointed at the sun).

Buzzer
Active · Sound Output · Actuator

A buzzer produces sound when electricity is applied. There are two types. A passive buzzer requires you to supply a frequency (it makes sound at whatever frequency you drive it at, using PWM — this is how you play different tones). An active buzzer has an internal oscillator and just buzzes at a fixed frequency when you apply power — simpler to use, less flexible.

For music and tones, use a passive buzzer with the Arduino's tone() function, which handles the PWM frequency for you. You can play any note by specifying its frequency in hertz. There are plenty of Arduino projects that play recognisable songs on a passive buzzer, from Nokia ringtones to video game themes — genuinely fun to experiment with.

For alarm systems, simple feedback, or status indicators, an active buzzer is fine and easier. Both types have polarity (longer leg = positive) and run directly from an Arduino digital pin without needing a transistor, since their current draw is low enough.

Relay
Active · High-Power Switching · Interface

A relay is an electrically controlled mechanical switch. Apply a small control voltage to its coil (5V from an Arduino, through a transistor) and an electromagnet pulls a physical metal contact closed, completing a separate circuit that can carry much higher voltages and currents.

This electrical isolation between the control side and the switched side is the key feature of a relay. It means a 5V Arduino can safely switch a 12V motor, or a 230V appliance (with proper safety precautions), without those high voltages ever touching the microcontroller.

Relays come as individual components or as module boards (typically blue, with one or more relay units, indicator LEDs, and transistor drivers built in). The module boards are much easier to use because the transistor driver and flyback diode protection are already built in.

They're slower than transistors (mechanical movement takes a few milliseconds) and they click audibly when switching, but for turning things on and off at relatively low frequencies, relays are robust and reliable.

IMU — MPU6050
Active · Motion Sensing · Sensor

The MPU6050 is an Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) — a sensor that simultaneously measures acceleration in three axes (X, Y, Z) using an accelerometer, and rotational rate in three axes using a gyroscope. Six measurements total. That's why these are called 6-DOF (six degrees of freedom) sensors.

The accelerometer detects linear movement and tilt (gravity is always pulling down, so by measuring which direction the gravitational pull is coming from, you can calculate the orientation of the sensor). The gyroscope measures how fast the sensor is rotating — not the angle itself, but the rate of change of angle.

Combining both (a technique called sensor fusion) lets you calculate the orientation of the sensor accurately and smoothly. The MPU6050 even has a built-in processor (the DMP — Digital Motion Processor) that can do the sensor fusion internally, sending you ready-computed orientation data over I2C.

This is the sensor inside your phone that knows which way it's tilted. It's inside drones, in self-balancing robots (like a two-wheeled robot that stays upright by constantly correcting its lean angle), in game controllers, in VR headsets. One of the most useful sensors you can add to any robot that moves.

Voltage Regulator
Active · Power Management · Infrastructure

A voltage regulator takes an input voltage (say, 12V from a battery) and outputs a lower, stable voltage (say, 5V) regardless of how the input fluctuates. This is critical because most microcontrollers and sensors need a clean, stable supply voltage. If the voltage they receive varies significantly, they behave erratically or get damaged.

The 7805 is the classic linear voltage regulator — it outputs a stable 5V from an input of 7V–35V. It's simple (three pins: input, ground, output) and bulletproof, but it wastes the excess voltage as heat. If you're dropping 12V to 5V and drawing 500mA, the 7805 is dissipating (12-5) × 0.5 = 3.5 watts as heat. It needs a heatsink.

Buck converters (also called switching regulators or step-down converters) are far more efficient. Instead of burning off excess voltage as heat, they rapidly switch the input on and off and filter the result to get a lower output voltage. Efficiency is typically 85–95% compared to 40–60% for a linear regulator. The LM2596 and MT3608 are popular modules. If you're running a robot off batteries and care about runtime, use a buck converter.

Push Button / Switch
Passive · User Input · Sensor

A push button is a simple momentary switch — it connects two terminals when pressed, disconnects when released. Nothing more. But it's one of the most fundamental ways a human can interact with a robot, and it appears in almost every beginner project.

Reading a button in Arduino code introduces an important concept: the pull-up or pull-down resistor. When a button is not pressed, the Arduino pin reading it is floating — connected to nothing, picking up electrical noise and giving random readings. A pull-up resistor connects the pin to 5V through a high-value resistor (typically 10kΩ), keeping it firmly HIGH when the button is open and LOW when the button is pressed (connecting it to GND). Arduino even has internal pull-up resistors you can enable in code without any external components.

Toggle switches, rocker switches, and DIP switches are variations on the same principle — they connect or disconnect a circuit. Limit switches (micro-switches with a small lever) are used to detect when a robot arm or moving part has reached the end of its travel range.

3.1 The SDA Loop, Revisited

Every component in Part A belongs to at least one of three categories in the SDA loop. Some components are pure sensors (they only gather information). Some are pure actuators (they only take action). Some are infrastructure (they don't directly sense or act, but they make everything else possible). And the microcontroller sits at the centre, deciding.

SDA Role Components
Sense Ultrasonic sensor, IR sensor, DHT11/22, soil moisture sensor, LDR, MPU6050, push button
Decide Microcontroller (Arduino Uno, Nano, ESP32)
Act DC motor, servo motor, LED, buzzer, OLED display, relay
Infrastructure Resistor, capacitor, transistor, diode, motor driver, voltage regulator

Infrastructure components don't sense or act on their own — they're the support system that makes sensing and acting possible at all. A resistor protecting an LED is infrastructure. A motor driver letting the Arduino control a motor is infrastructure. A capacitor smoothing the power supply is infrastructure. Without them, the active components don't work properly or don't last long.

3.2 Signal Flow: How Information Moves Through a Robot

In a real robot, information flows in one direction through the SDA loop and then loops back. Understanding that flow — what type of signal goes between which components and in which direction — is what lets you read a schematic or design your own circuit without guessing.

Sense →
Sensor to Microcontroller

Sensors produce one of two types of output: analog or digital. An analog sensor (like an LDR or soil moisture sensor) produces a voltage between 0V and the supply voltage — any value in that range is possible. The Arduino's analog input pins (A0–A5) convert that voltage into a number between 0 and 1023 using a built-in ADC (Analog to Digital Converter). A reading of 512 means roughly half of the supply voltage.

A digital sensor (like a push button or a simple IR proximity module) produces either HIGH or LOW — full voltage or zero. The Arduino reads this on a digital input pin as 1 or 0 in code.

Some sensors (like the HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensor and DHT11 temperature sensor) use a simple digital communication protocol where they send timed pulses that encode the measurement. Others (like the MPU6050 and OLED display) use I2C — a two-wire bus where multiple components can share the same two wires and the microcontroller addresses each one individually.

Decide →
Microcontroller: Reading and Deciding

The microcontroller reads all its inputs (sensors, buttons, communication buses), runs the code you've written, and determines what outputs to set. This happens in a loop — the Arduino's main loop() function runs continuously, checking inputs and updating outputs hundreds or thousands of times per second.

In code, a decision looks like this: if the ultrasonic sensor reads less than 20cm, set the motor driver pins to stop. Otherwise, set them to move forward. That's the entire logic of an obstacle-avoiding robot, condensed to two lines. Everything else is just wiring and setup.

The microcontroller's output pins can be set HIGH (5V) or LOW (0V). For analog-ish control (motor speed, LED brightness), PWM-capable pins can rapidly switch between HIGH and LOW at a frequency the component can't follow, effectively delivering a fraction of the full voltage.

Act →
Microcontroller to Actuators

The microcontroller's output goes to actuators, usually through some kind of interface component. For LEDs and buzzers (low current), the pin drives them directly (with a current-limiting resistor for the LED). For motors (high current), the pin signal goes to a motor driver, which handles the actual motor power. For relays, the pin signal goes through a transistor driver (usually built into the relay module) which activates the relay coil.

The physical result — a wheel spinning, a light turning on, a buzzer sounding — is the Act step. And then the sensors immediately start measuring again, the loop continues, and the robot responds to whatever just changed in its environment.

3.4 Component Interactions You Need to Know

A few specific component relationships come up so often in robotics that they're worth spelling out explicitly.

Relationship 01 — Microcontroller + Motor Driver + Motor The microcontroller cannot power a motor. It can only signal. The motor driver receives the signal and switches the motor's power on/off or changes direction. Never skip the motor driver. The Arduino pin will burn out quickly and silently if you try to drive a motor directly.
Relationship 02 — Sensor + Pull-up/Pull-down Resistor Many digital sensors and buttons leave the microcontroller input floating (indeterminate) when they're not actively signalling. A pull-up resistor (to 5V) or pull-down resistor (to GND) holds the pin at a known value when the sensor is idle. This prevents random noise from triggering false readings. If your sensor is giving erratic readings for no apparent reason, a missing pull-up or pull-down is usually the cause.
Relationship 03 — Motor + Flyback Diode Every motor (and every relay coil, and every solenoid) is an inductive load. When you cut power to an inductive component, the collapsing magnetic field generates a brief reverse voltage spike that can destroy transistors and microcontroller pins. A flyback diode placed across the motor terminals in reverse polarity gives that spike a safe path. Always include flyback diodes when driving inductive loads. Motor driver modules usually have them built in — check the datasheet to confirm.
Relationship 04 — Power Supply + Decoupling Capacitor Every microcontroller and many sensors need a decoupling capacitor (typically 100nF ceramic) placed as physically close as possible to their power pin. When the chip's internal logic switches rapidly, it draws brief current spikes from the supply. The decoupling capacitor sits right there and absorbs those spikes before they propagate backwards down the power line and cause interference with other components. You'll see this in every professional circuit board — it's not optional once you start building anything that needs to be reliable.

4.1 How a DC Motor Works

A DC motor converts electrical energy into mechanical rotation using electromagnetism. Inside the motor there's a rotor (the part that spins, wrapped in coils of wire) sitting inside a stator (the stationary outer part, which contains permanent magnets). When current flows through the rotor coils, they become electromagnets. Those electromagnets are repelled and attracted by the permanent magnets in the stator, and that force causes the rotor to spin.

The rotor keeps spinning because the direction of current through the coils reverses at exactly the right moment (handled by a component called the commutator and brushes) so the electromagnetic forces always push in the same rotational direction. This is why DC motors are also called brushed motors — the brushes make physical contact with the commutator to switch the current.

Brushless DC motors (used in drones, electric vehicles, and high-end robotics) don't have brushes — the current switching is done electronically instead. They're more efficient and longer-lasting but require more complex control circuitry. We use brushed DC motors in this course because they're simpler and cheaper.

Three things control a DC motor: voltage (determines maximum speed), current (determines torque), and the direction of current flow (determines rotation direction). That's the whole picture.

Reverse the polarity (swap which terminal gets positive and which gets negative) and the motor spins the other way. This is the most basic fact of DC motor control, and the H-bridge circuit exists specifically to do this reversal electronically, without touching any wires.

4.2 Speed Control: PWM

If you connect a motor directly to a fixed voltage (say, 6V from four AA batteries), it runs at one speed. To make it run slower, you need less effective voltage. You could use a resistor to drop the voltage, but that wastes energy as heat and the speed changes as the motor load changes. The better way is PWM.

PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) rapidly switches the motor's power on and off, many times per second. The motor can't respond fast enough to actually stop and start with each switch — instead it effectively sees the average voltage. If the signal is on 50% of the time and off 50% of the time (50% duty cycle), the motor behaves as if it's receiving half the voltage. At 25% duty cycle, it behaves as if it's receiving a quarter of the voltage.

On an Arduino, PWM is output using the analogWrite(pin, value) function. The value goes from 0 (always off, 0% duty cycle) to 255 (always on, 100% duty cycle). So to run a motor at half speed, you write analogWrite(motorPin, 127). At three-quarter speed: analogWrite(motorPin, 191).

PWM only works on specific pins. On the Arduino Uno, pins 3, 5, 6, 9, 10, and 11 are PWM-capable (marked with a tilde ~ symbol on the board). Using analogWrite() on a non-PWM pin does nothing useful.

The dead band problem Not all duty cycles actually move the motor. Below a certain threshold (which varies by motor, load, and gearing), the signal isn't strong enough to overcome static friction and the motor just sits there humming. This threshold is called the dead band. If your motor doesn't move until you hit analogWrite(pin, 80), then values 0–79 are your dead band. Always test your specific motor to find where it actually starts moving.

The L298N module (the motor driver from Module 03) contains two H-bridges internally, allowing it to control two DC motors independently. It has dedicated input pins for each motor that you control from the Arduino — setting them HIGH or LOW changes which H-bridge switches are open and closed.

The common ground connection deserves emphasis. The Arduino and the motor driver must share a common GND reference. Even a small DC motor can draw 300–500mA running freely and much more under load. Power motors from a separate battery. Use the motor driver as the interface. Connect grounds together. That's the safe pattern.

4.5 Controlling the Motor in Code

Once the L298N is wired correctly, controlling the motor from code is straightforward. The logic is: set IN1 and IN2 to determine direction, set ENA to determine speed.

// Pin definitions const int IN1 = 8; const int IN2 = 9; const int ENA = 10; // Must be a PWM pin (~) void setup() { pinMode(IN1, OUTPUT); pinMode(IN2, OUTPUT); pinMode(ENA, OUTPUT); } void loop() { // Forward at 75% speed digitalWrite(IN1, HIGH); digitalWrite(IN2, LOW); analogWrite(ENA, 191); // 191/255 ≈ 75% delay(2000); // Run for 2 seconds // Brake (stop) digitalWrite(IN1, LOW); digitalWrite(IN2, LOW); analogWrite(ENA, 0); delay(1000); // Reverse at half speed digitalWrite(IN1, LOW); digitalWrite(IN2, HIGH); analogWrite(ENA, 127); // 127/255 ≈ 50% delay(2000); // Stop again analogWrite(ENA, 0); delay(1000); }

Read through that code top to bottom. You don't need to fully understand Arduino syntax yet (that's Module 5). What matters right now is recognising the pattern: direction is set by HIGH/LOW combinations on IN1 and IN2, speed is set by the value in analogWrite(). That pattern stays consistent across almost every motor driver chip you'll ever use — only the pin names change.

4.6 Servo Motors: Precise Positioning

A DC motor with PWM speed control gives you continuous rotation with variable speed. A servo motor gives you something different: precise angular positioning. Tell it to go to 45 degrees, it goes to 45 degrees and holds there against external force. Tell it to go to 135 degrees, it moves smoothly to 135 degrees.

The servo achieves this using a built-in feedback loop. Inside the servo housing there's a DC motor, a gearbox, a position sensor (a potentiometer — a variable resistor that changes with shaft angle), and a small control circuit. The control circuit constantly compares the desired position (received from your PWM signal) with the actual position (measured by the potentiometer) and drives the motor to close the gap. This is called a closed-loop control system — and it's the same fundamental principle used in industrial robots, CNC machines, and autopilot systems.

Servo motors are controlled by a PWM signal with a very specific timing. The signal period is 20ms (50Hz). A pulse width of 1ms corresponds to 0 degrees. A pulse width of 2ms corresponds to 180 degrees. Everything in between maps proportionally. You don't need to calculate this yourself — the Arduino Servo library handles it entirely.

// Servo control — requires the Servo library #include <Servo.h> Servo myServo; void setup() { myServo.attach(9); // Connect servo signal wire to pin 9 } void loop() { myServo.write(0); // Go to 0 degrees delay(1000); myServo.write(90); // Go to 90 degrees (centre) delay(1000); myServo.write(180); // Go to 180 degrees delay(1000); }

Three lines of actual logic. The Servo library handles all the PWM timing behind the scenes. This is one of the most beginner-friendly things in all of Arduino — you write a single number (the angle) and the motor goes there.

In robotics, servos appear in robotic arms (each joint is a servo), in pan-tilt camera mounts (two servos, one for left-right, one for up-down), in steering mechanisms, in gripper mechanisms on robot arms, and anywhere that you need a component to move to and hold a specific position.

Powering Servos Correctly Do not power servos from the Arduino's 5V pin unless you're using a single micro servo (like the SG90) with no load. Servos draw significant current, especially when moving or holding against a load. Power the servo's red wire directly from a 5V supply (or from your battery through a 5V regulator). Connect the servo's black wire to the common ground. Connect only the signal wire (orange or yellow) to the Arduino pin. This three-wire pattern is standard across all hobby servos.

4.7 Stepper Motors: Counting Steps

Stepper motors are a different breed. Instead of spinning freely (like a DC motor) or moving to an angle (like a servo), a stepper motor moves in discrete, fixed increments called steps. Each step is a fraction of a full rotation — commonly 1.8 degrees per step, meaning 200 steps per full revolution.

The key difference from a servo is that stepper motors are open-loop — they don't have a built-in position sensor. They assume that if you commanded 100 steps, 100 steps happened. If the motor skipped steps due to excessive load, the controller doesn't know. This is why stepper motors are used in applications where the load is predictable and controlled: 3D printers, CNC machines, camera sliders, telescope mounts.

Inside a stepper motor are multiple electromagnet coils arranged around the rotor. By energising them in a specific sequence, the rotor is pulled from one position to the next. The sequence of which coils get power and in which order determines direction and speed.

Stepper Motor — 4-Step Full Step Sequence (Unipolar)
Step Coil A Coil B Coil C Coil D
1 HIGH LOW LOW LOW
2 LOW HIGH LOW LOW
3 LOW LOW HIGH LOW
4 LOW LOW LOW HIGH

Repeat this sequence to rotate forward. Reverse the sequence to rotate backward. Repeating steps 1–4 in order moves the motor one full step at a time.

The most common beginner stepper motor is the 28BYJ-48, which comes with a matching ULN2003 driver board. This combination is cheap, widely available, and well-documented. The 28BYJ-48 has a built-in 1:64 gearbox, giving it good torque but relatively slow speed — fine for camera sliders, clock mechanisms, and small positioning systems.

For higher performance (faster, more torque, more precision), the NEMA 17 stepper motor paired with an A4988 or DRV8825 driver is the industry standard for 3D printers and CNC machines. Understanding the 28BYJ-48 first gives you everything you need to understand the NEMA 17 later.

When to use which motor DC motor: Continuous rotation, variable speed, simple direction control. Wheels, fans, pumps. Use when you don't need to know exactly how far it turned.

Servo motor: Precise angular positioning within a limited range (0–180°). Robot joints, steering, camera mounts. Use when you need it to go to and hold a specific angle.

Stepper motor: Precise, repeatable positioning with counting. 3D printers, CNC, linear actuators. Use when exact position control over many rotations matters more than speed.

4.8 Differential Steering: How a Two-Wheeled Robot Turns

Most wheeled robots (and all the ones you'll build in this course) use differential steering — the same system used in tanks, electric wheelchairs, and tracked vehicles. There's no steering wheel. Instead, the left and right motors are controlled independently, and steering happens by varying the speed or direction difference between the two sides.

Left motor Right motor Robot behaviour
Forward, full speed Forward, full speed Straight ahead
Forward, full speed Forward, half speed Gentle curve to the right
Forward, full speed Stopped Sharp turn to the right (pivot around right wheel)
Forward, full speed Reverse, full speed Spin on the spot (pivot turn)
Stopped Stopped Stationary
Reverse, full speed Reverse, full speed Straight back

The sharpness of a turn is determined by the speed difference between the two sides. A small difference produces a wide, gentle arc. A large difference (one side stopped or reversed) produces a tight turn or a pivot. This gives you complete, smooth steering control using only two values: left motor speed and right motor speed.

With PWM control on both motors, you can implement proportional steering — the faster you want to turn, the more you increase the speed difference. This is the foundation of more advanced control algorithms like PID (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) control, which is how self-driving vehicles keep themselves on track with smooth, stable steering. You don't need PID yet. But the differential speed difference idea is the seed of it.

4.9 Two-Motor Code: The Rover Movement Functions

With two motors and an L298N controlling both (using IN1/IN2/ENA for motor A and IN3/IN4/ENB for motor B), here's what the movement function set looks like. This is essentially the movement layer of the Module 7 rover — you're seeing it early.

// Motor A (left wheel) const int IN1 = 8, IN2 = 9, ENA = 10; // Motor B (right wheel) const int IN3 = 7, IN4 = 6, ENB = 5; void moveForward(int speed) { digitalWrite(IN1, HIGH); digitalWrite(IN2, LOW); digitalWrite(IN3, HIGH); digitalWrite(IN4, LOW); analogWrite(ENA, speed); analogWrite(ENB, speed); } void moveReverse(int speed) { digitalWrite(IN1, LOW); digitalWrite(IN2, HIGH); digitalWrite(IN3, LOW); digitalWrite(IN4, HIGH); analogWrite(ENA, speed); analogWrite(ENB, speed); } void turnRight(int speed) { digitalWrite(IN1, HIGH); digitalWrite(IN2, LOW); // Left forward digitalWrite(IN3, LOW); digitalWrite(IN4, HIGH); // Right reverse analogWrite(ENA, speed); analogWrite(ENB, speed); } void turnLeft(int speed) { digitalWrite(IN1, LOW); digitalWrite(IN2, HIGH); // Left reverse digitalWrite(IN3, HIGH); digitalWrite(IN4, LOW); // Right forward analogWrite(ENA, speed); analogWrite(ENB, speed); } void stopMotors() { analogWrite(ENA, 0); analogWrite(ENB, 0); }

These five functions are the complete movement vocabulary of a two-wheeled robot. In Module 7, you'll call them based on what the ultrasonic sensor is reading. The logic of the rover is almost entirely in those calls: if distance is less than 20cm, call stopMotors(), then moveReverse(), then turnRight(), then moveForward(). That's obstacle avoidance.

4.10 Motor Protection: What Goes Wrong and How to Stop It

Motors fail. Motor drivers fail. Understanding the failure modes before they happen means fewer destroyed components and less frustration.

Failure Mode 01 — Stall Current When a motor's shaft is physically blocked from turning, it draws its maximum current (stall current). For a typical small DC motor, this can be 5–10× the normal running current. Sustained stall current heats the motor windings, the motor driver, and potentially melts wiring. If your robot gets stuck against a wall and keeps trying to push, the motors are stalling. Add a current timeout in software: if the robot hasn't moved after a certain time, back off. Module 6 covers sensing the stall condition using current monitoring.
Failure Mode 02 — Voltage Spikes (Back-EMF) When a motor is running and you suddenly cut power, the magnetic field in the motor coils collapses and generates a reverse voltage spike (back-EMF). Without flyback diodes, this spike travels back into the motor driver and can destroy the output transistors. The L298N has internal flyback diodes but they're marginal for larger motors. For motors drawing more than about 1A, add external 1N4007 diodes across each motor terminal to GND and to the supply voltage.
Failure Mode 03 — Shared Ground Missing If the Arduino and motor driver don't share a common ground, the Arduino's HIGH signal (5V relative to Arduino's GND) is meaningless to the motor driver (which has its own separate GND reference). The motor driver sees no consistent reference and behaves erratically or not at all. Always connect Arduino GND to motor driver GND. Always.
Failure Mode 04 — Motor Power from Arduino Pin Connecting a motor directly to an Arduino output pin instead of through a motor driver will burn out the pin. Arduino output pins are rated for 40mA maximum. Most motors draw 10–20× that. The pin will get hot, the Arduino's output driver will fail, and in some cases the entire microcontroller gets damaged. Always use a motor driver. No exceptions.

4.11 Gears, Wheels, and Physical Design

The electronics and code are only half the story. A robot that moves is also a mechanical system, and the mechanical design determines whether your electronics can actually do their job.

Wheel diameter matters. A larger wheel covers more ground per revolution. If your motor runs at 150 RPM and your wheel is 65mm in diameter, the robot moves at approximately 150 × π × 0.065 = 30.6 metres per minute (about 0.5 m/s). Change the wheel to 130mm diameter and you double the speed without changing the motor or the code.

Gearing determines the torque-speed trade-off. A high gear ratio (like 1:100 in a gearbox) multiplies torque by 100 but divides speed by 100. Use a high gear ratio for robots carrying heavy loads or climbing inclines. Use a lower ratio for fast, light robots on flat surfaces.

Wheel contact and traction. A wheeled robot with smooth wheels on a smooth surface will slip, especially when accelerating or turning. Rubber tyres increase grip. Wider wheels spread the load and reduce slipping. If your robot slides unpredictably in turns, it's usually a traction problem, not a code problem.

Weight distribution. On a two-wheeled robot with a caster at the front or back, where the heavy components (battery, especially) sit relative to the drive wheels affects how the robot handles. Too much weight behind the drive wheels and the front caster lifts. Too much in front and the caster drags heavily, increasing friction and reducing turning ability.

5.0 Why Arduino, and Why C++

The Arduino platform uses a simplified version of C++. If that sounds intimidating, it shouldn't — Arduino strips out the complicated parts of C++ and leaves you with a clean, readable language that's genuinely accessible to beginners while being powerful enough for serious projects.

Why not Python? Python is a great language and you probably already know some. But Python runs on a computer's operating system — it needs an OS underneath it, RAM to work with, and processing power that a simple microcontroller doesn't have. Arduino's C++ compiles directly to machine code that runs bare-metal on the ATmega328P chip. That's what allows a chip smaller than your thumbnail to respond to sensor input in microseconds and run for months on a battery.

The ESP32 and Raspberry Pi Pico can both run MicroPython (a Python variant for microcontrollers) if you want to work in Python eventually. But starting on Arduino with C++ builds habits and intuitions that transfer everywhere — to Python, to Rust, to assembly if you ever go that deep. The logic is the same. Only the syntax changes.

Code is how you tell the microcontroller what to do with the information its sensors gather. Without code, you have hardware. With code, you have a robot.

5.1 The Structure of Every Arduino Sketch

Every Arduino program — called a sketch — follows the same structure. There are no exceptions. Two functions, always present, always named the same way.

Global section Above both functions. This is where you include libraries, define pin numbers, and declare variables that need to be accessible throughout the entire program. Code here runs before anything else, at compile time.
setup() Runs once, immediately when the Arduino powers on or resets. Used for one-time configuration: setting pin modes, initialising the Serial monitor, starting libraries. After setup() finishes, it never runs again.
loop() Runs continuously, over and over, as long as the Arduino has power. This is where your robot's actual behaviour lives — reading sensors, making decisions, controlling actuators. Each pass through loop() is one iteration of the SDA loop.
Your functions Below loop(), or anywhere outside setup() and loop(). Custom functions you define — moveForward(), readDistance(), checkObstacle(). Called from loop() or from other functions.
// Global section — libraries, pin definitions, variables const int LED_PIN = 13; int counter = 0; void setup() { pinMode(LED_PIN, OUTPUT); // Configure pin 13 as output Serial.begin(9600); // Start Serial monitor at 9600 baud } void loop() { digitalWrite(LED_PIN, HIGH); // Turn LED on delay(500); // Wait 500ms digitalWrite(LED_PIN, LOW); // Turn LED off delay(500); // Wait 500ms counter++; // Increment counter Serial.println(counter); // Print counter to Serial monitor }

This is Blink — the Hello World of Arduino. The LED on pin 13 (built into every Arduino Uno board) flashes on and off every half second, and the Serial monitor counts how many times it has blinked. It's simple, but it exercises the core structure: global variable, setup() configuring pins, loop() doing the repeated work.

If you have an Arduino, upload this now. Watch the LED blink. Open the Serial monitor (Tools → Serial Monitor in the Arduino IDE, set baud rate to 9600). Watch the counter increment. That counter going up with every blink is the loop() function running — you're watching the SDA loop tick in real time.

5.2 Variables and Data Types

A variable is a named location in memory that holds a value. You give it a name, a type, and an initial value. Every time you use the name in code, you're accessing that memory location.

Type What it holds Range / Notes
int Whole numbers -32,768 to 32,767. The most common type in Arduino code.
long Large whole numbers -2,147,483,648 to 2,147,483,647. Use for millis() values, which can get large.
float Decimal numbers Up to 7 significant digits. Slow on Arduino — avoid in time-critical code.
bool True or false Only two values: true (1) and false (0). Used for flags and conditions.
char A single character One letter, number, or symbol. Written in single quotes: 'A', '3', '?'.

5.3 Conditionals: Making Decisions

The if statement is the decision-making structure of any program. It checks a condition and runs a block of code only if that condition is true. The else clause runs if the condition is false. else if chains let you check multiple conditions in sequence.

if (distance < 20) { // This runs if distance is less than 20cm stopMotors(); Serial.println("Obstacle detected — stopping"); } else if (distance < 40) { // Between 20cm and 40cm — slow down moveForward(100); Serial.println("Close — slowing"); } else { // More than 40cm — full speed moveForward(200); }

Read that top to bottom. The robot checks distance. If it's under 20cm, stop. If it's between 20 and 40cm, slow down. If it's more than 40cm, go full speed. Three behaviours from twelve lines of logic. That's the Decide step of the SDA loop, written in code.

Comparison operators you'll use constantly:

Operator Meaning Example
== Equal to if (buttonState == HIGH)
!= Not equal to if (mode != 0)
< Less than if (distance < 20)
> Greater than if (temperature > 35)
<= Less than or equal to if (moisture <= 400)
&& AND — both conditions must be true if (distance < 20 && speed > 0)
|| OR — at least one condition must be true if (leftSensor == LOW || rightSensor == LOW)
! NOT — inverts a boolean if (!obstacleDetected)
The single most common bug in all of programming: using = (assignment) instead of == (comparison) inside an if statement. if (x = 5) doesn't check whether x is 5 — it sets x to 5 and then evaluates to true (because 5 is non-zero). The robot does the wrong thing and you spend an hour wondering why. Always use == for comparisons.

5.4 Loops: Repeating Things

The Arduino's main loop() runs continuously — that's one kind of loop. But inside your code, you often need to repeat something a specific number of times or until a condition changes. That's what for and while loops are for.

// for loop — repeat a fixed number of times for (int i = 0; i < 5; i++) { digitalWrite(LED_PIN, HIGH); delay(100); digitalWrite(LED_PIN, LOW); delay(100); } // LED blinks exactly 5 times, then continues // while loop — repeat until condition becomes false while (distance < 20) { stopMotors(); distance = readDistance(); // Keep measuring } // Exits the while loop only when distance >= 20 moveForward(200);

The for loop structure has three parts: initialisation (int i = 0), condition (i < 5), and increment (i++). It runs as long as the condition is true, incrementing i each time. When i hits 5, the condition fails and the loop exits.

The while loop keeps running as long as its condition is true. Be careful with while loops — if the condition never becomes false, the loop runs forever and the rest of your program freezes. In the example above, if the ultrasonic sensor stops working and always returns 0, the robot will stop and never move again, stuck in the while loop forever. Always make sure your while loop conditions can actually become false.

5.5 Functions: Organising Your Code

A function is a named block of code that does one specific thing. You define it once, then call it by name wherever you need it. Functions are how you keep loop() readable — instead of 200 lines of sensor reads, motor controls, and decision logic all tangled together, you break it into named functions that each do one thing cleanly.

// A function that returns a value long readDistance() { digitalWrite(TRIG_PIN, LOW); delayMicroseconds(2); digitalWrite(TRIG_PIN, HIGH); delayMicroseconds(10); digitalWrite(TRIG_PIN, LOW); long duration = pulseIn(ECHO_PIN, HIGH); long distance = duration / 58; // Convert to centimetres return distance; } // A function that doesn't return a value (void) void handleObstacle() { stopMotors(); delay(300); moveReverse(180); delay(500); turnRight(180); delay(400); } // loop() stays clean and readable void loop() { int dist = readDistance(); if (dist < DISTANCE_THRESHOLD) { handleObstacle(); } else { moveForward(200); } }

Notice how loop() is now five lines. It reads the distance, checks it, and calls the right function. You can read it and understand the robot's behaviour at a glance. The complexity is still there — it's just organised into functions where it belongs.

Functions can take parameters (inputs) and return values (outputs). readDistance() takes no parameters and returns a long (the distance in cm). moveForward(speed) takes an int (the speed value) and returns nothing (void). The return type goes before the function name. If the function returns nothing, the type is void.

5.6 The Serial Monitor: Your Best Debugging Tool

When something doesn't work — and it will not work, frequently, especially at first — your first question is always: what is the robot actually seeing? What value is that sensor returning? What does the code think the distance is?

The Serial monitor answers those questions. It's a text window that displays messages sent from the Arduino over the USB cable. You print to it like this:

Serial.begin(9600); // In setup() — start Serial at 9600 baud // In loop() or functions: Serial.print("Distance: "); // Print without newline Serial.print(distance); // Print the variable value Serial.println(" cm"); // Print with newline at end // Output in Serial monitor: "Distance: 34 cm"

Use the Serial monitor constantly while developing. Print every sensor reading. Print every decision the robot makes. Print the value of variables you're not sure about. Once your code works reliably, you can remove or comment out the Serial prints — they do slow the code slightly because sending data over USB takes time.

The baud rate (9600 in the example) must match between your Serial.begin() call and the Serial monitor's baud rate selector dropdown. Mismatched baud rates produce garbled text. 9600 is the safe default for beginners. 115200 is faster and fine for most projects.

Serial Monitor Trick Serial.print() is the most powerful debugging tool you have and it costs nothing to use. The single habit that separates programmers who debug quickly from those who guess for hours is this: when something doesn't work, print everything. Distance readings, variable states, which branch of an if statement ran, how many times the loop has executed. Make the invisible visible. Fix the problem. Remove the prints. Ship the code.

5.7 Reading Sensors in Code

The Sense step of the SDA loop, in code. Here's how to read the most common sensors you'll encounter in this course.

HC-SR04 Ultrasonic Sensor — Distance Reading

The HC-SR04 requires a specific trigger sequence: pull Trig LOW for 2µs, then HIGH for 10µs, then LOW again. This fires a sonic pulse. Then you measure how long Echo stays HIGH — that duration, divided by 58, gives distance in centimetres.

const int TRIG = 7; const int ECHO = 8; void setup() { pinMode(TRIG, OUTPUT); pinMode(ECHO, INPUT); Serial.begin(9600); } long getDistance() { digitalWrite(TRIG, LOW); delayMicroseconds(2); digitalWrite(TRIG, HIGH); delayMicroseconds(10); digitalWrite(TRIG, LOW); long duration = pulseIn(ECHO, HIGH); return duration / 58; } void loop() { long dist = getDistance(); Serial.print("Distance: "); Serial.print(dist); Serial.println(" cm"); delay(100); }
DHT11 Temperature & Humidity — Library-Based Reading

The DHT library handles all the timing complexity. Install it via Arduino IDE's Library Manager (Tools → Manage Libraries → search "DHT sensor library" by Adafruit). Then it's three lines to get temperature and humidity.

#include <DHT.h> const int DHT_PIN = 4; DHT dht(DHT_PIN, DHT11); void setup() { Serial.begin(9600); dht.begin(); } void loop() { float temp = dht.readTemperature(); float humidity = dht.readHumidity(); Serial.print("Temp: "); Serial.print(temp); Serial.print("°C Humidity: "); Serial.println(humidity); delay(2000); // DHT11 needs at least 1s between readings }
LDR (Light Sensor) — Analog Reading

The LDR paired with a fixed resistor forms a voltage divider on an analog pin. analogRead() returns 0–1023. High values mean bright (low resistance), low values mean dark (high resistance) — though this depends on which way you've wired the voltage divider.

const int LDR_PIN = A0; // Analog pin const int DARK_THRESHOLD = 300; // Below this = dark room void setup() { Serial.begin(9600); } void loop() { int lightLevel = analogRead(LDR_PIN); Serial.print("Light: "); Serial.println(lightLevel); if (lightLevel < DARK_THRESHOLD) { Serial.println("Dark — turn on light"); // Trigger your actuator here } delay(200); }

Notice the pattern across all three: define pins, configure in setup(), read in loop(), act on the reading. The sensor changes. The pattern doesn't. Once you've read one sensor in code, reading the next one feels familiar immediately.

5.8 delay() vs millis(): The Timing Problem

delay() is simple and beginner-friendly. It pauses everything for the specified number of milliseconds. But "pauses everything" is the problem — while the Arduino is in a delay, it cannot read sensors, check buttons, update outputs, or do anything else. For a simple blink sketch, that's fine. For a robot that needs to check its distance sensor 10 times per second while also watching for a button press and updating an OLED display, delay() will make it feel broken and unresponsive.

The solution is millis(). Instead of pausing, you record the time something happened and check whether enough time has passed on each pass through loop(). The loop keeps running — fast, hundreds of times per second — and each piece of timing logic checks the clock independently.

// Blinking an LED without delay() — non-blocking const int LED_PIN = 13; const long BLINK_INTERVAL = 500; // ms between blinks unsigned long lastBlink = 0; bool ledState = false; void setup() { pinMode(LED_PIN, OUTPUT); } void loop() { unsigned long now = millis(); // Check if 500ms has passed since last blink if (now - lastBlink >= BLINK_INTERVAL) { lastBlink = now; ledState = !ledState; // Toggle state digitalWrite(LED_PIN, ledState); } // Everything else still runs at full speed // — read sensors, check buttons, update display }

This is more code than the delay() version. But the robot is now fully responsive at all times. The blink timer runs without blocking anything else. This pattern (record a timestamp, check elapsed time, act, update timestamp) appears in almost every real robotics project. Learn it now and it'll save you hours of confusion later.

5.9 State Machines: Thinking in Behaviours

A state machine is a way of organising robot behaviour into distinct states, where the robot is always in exactly one state at a time and transitions between states based on sensor input or events.

The obstacle-avoiding rover has four states:

MOVING_FORWARD
Both motors forward at full speed. Transitions to STOPPING if obstacle detected within threshold.
STOPPING
Both motors stopped. Waits 300ms. Transitions to REVERSING automatically.
REVERSING
Both motors reverse. Waits 500ms. Transitions to TURNING automatically.
TURNING
Left motor forward, right motor reverse (pivot right). Waits 400ms. Transitions to MOVING_FORWARD.
// State machine for obstacle-avoiding rover enum RoverState { MOVING_FORWARD, STOPPING, REVERSING, TURNING }; RoverState currentState = MOVING_FORWARD; unsigned long stateStartTime = 0; void loop() { long dist = getDistance(); unsigned long now = millis(); switch (currentState) { case MOVING_FORWARD: moveForward(200); if (dist < DISTANCE_THRESHOLD) { currentState = STOPPING; stateStartTime = now; } break; case STOPPING: stopMotors(); if (now - stateStartTime >= 300) { currentState = REVERSING; stateStartTime = now; } break; case REVERSING: moveReverse(180); if (now - stateStartTime >= 500) { currentState = TURNING; stateStartTime = now; } break; case TURNING: turnRight(180); if (now - stateStartTime >= 400) { currentState = MOVING_FORWARD; } break; } }

This is the complete decision logic of the Module 7 rover, minus the motor and sensor function definitions (which you already have from Modules 04 and the sensor reading code above). The state machine structure makes it easy to read, easy to modify, and easy to add new behaviours — just add a new state and define when to transition into and out of it.

State machines appear everywhere in real engineering — traffic light controllers, vending machines, communication protocols, video game AI, autopilot systems. The rover is a simple example of a pattern that scales to enormous complexity.

5.11 Common Beginner Code Mistakes

These are not edge cases. They are the bugs that every beginner hits, usually multiple times. Knowing them in advance doesn't guarantee you'll avoid them, but it means you'll recognise them faster when they happen.

  • = instead of == in a condition. Sets a value instead of comparing. Almost always evaluates as true. Covered in 5.3 but worth repeating.
  • Missing semicolon. Every statement in C++ ends with a semicolon. Miss one and the compiler error message points to the line after the missing one, which is confusing. Always check the line above where the error points.
  • Mismatched braces. Every opening { needs a closing }. The IDE highlights matching braces when you click on one — use this constantly to check your structure.
  • int overflow with millis(). Using int instead of unsigned long for millis() values. Works fine for the first 32 seconds, then breaks silently. Always use unsigned long.
  • analogWrite() on a non-PWM pin. Nothing happens, no error. Check the board diagram — only pins marked ~ support PWM.
  • Forgetting pinMode(). Reading a pin you haven't configured as INPUT, or writing to one you haven't configured as OUTPUT. The read returns garbage, the write does nothing. Always set pinMode() for every pin you use in setup().
  • Using delay() in a complex build. The robot becomes unresponsive. Sensor readings are missed. Use millis() for timing in any project with more than one thing happening at once.
  • Variable scope confusion. A variable declared inside a function doesn't exist outside it. If you need a variable to persist between loop() iterations, declare it globally at the top of the sketch.

Sensors: Feeling the World

A robot without sensors is just a machine running a fixed script. Sensors are what make it responsive — what connect it to reality. This module goes deep on how they work, what they return, and how to make sense of messy data.

~40 min read · Serial monitor open helps

Terms Used in This Module

Skim before starting. Come back when something in the lesson throws you off.

Transducer Any device that converts one form of energy into another. All sensors are transducers — they convert physical phenomena (light, sound, heat, pressure) into electrical signals.
Resolution The smallest change a sensor can detect. A sensor with 10-bit resolution can distinguish 1024 different values across its range. Higher resolution means finer measurements.
Accuracy How close the sensor's reading is to the true value. A temperature sensor accurate to ±2°C may read 27°C when the actual temperature is anywhere from 25°C to 29°C.
Precision How consistent a sensor's readings are under the same conditions. A precise sensor gives the same reading every time — even if that reading is wrong. Accuracy and precision are different things.
Noise Random variation in a sensor's output that doesn't correspond to a real change in what's being measured. Every sensor produces some noise. Filtering reduces it.
Calibration The process of adjusting a sensor's readings to match known reference values. A soil moisture sensor needs calibration to know what "dry" and "wet" actually mean in terms of raw numbers.
ADC (Analog to Digital Converter) A circuit that converts a continuous analog voltage into a discrete digital number. The Arduino Uno's ADC is 10-bit, producing values from 0 to 1023. Built into the chip — used automatically when you call analogRead().

6.0 What a Sensor Actually Does

A sensor measures something physical and converts it into an electrical signal. That's the complete description. The physical thing could be light intensity, sound pressure, distance, temperature, humidity, magnetic field strength, acceleration, rotation, touch, gas concentration — anything in the observable world that changes electrically in response to a physical stimulus.

The electrical signal that comes out is either analog (a voltage that varies continuously across a range) or digital (a precise HIGH or LOW, or a timed sequence of pulses encoding a value). Module 05 covered how to read both types in Arduino code. This module goes deeper — into what the sensor is actually measuring, what can go wrong with that measurement, and how to handle the data once you have it.

The reason this matters is that sensor data is almost never clean. Real-world measurements are noisy, inconsistent, and sometimes outright wrong. A robot that acts directly on raw sensor data without any processing will twitch, stutter, and behave erratically. Learning to handle that messiness is a significant part of what makes a real robotics build work reliably.

The difference between a robot that works and a robot that works reliably is almost always in how it handles its sensor data — not in how it handles everything going right.

6.1 Analog vs Digital Output — In More Detail

You've seen this distinction before, but it's worth going deeper now that you're about to read actual sensor datasheets.

An analog sensor outputs a voltage that varies continuously with the physical quantity being measured. An LDR in a voltage divider might output 0.2V in bright sunlight and 4.8V in complete darkness — and any value in between for any intermediate light level. The Arduino's ADC samples this voltage and maps it to a number between 0 and 1023. The mapping is linear: 0V → 0, 2.5V → 511, 5V → 1023.

The resolution of this mapping is 5V ÷ 1024 steps = approximately 4.9mV per step. Any voltage change smaller than 4.9mV won't be detected — the ADC can't distinguish it. For most sensor applications this is fine. For precise measurements (like reading a thermocouple for high-accuracy temperature monitoring), you'd need an external ADC with higher resolution — 12-bit (4096 steps) or 16-bit (65536 steps).

A digital sensor outputs either a clean HIGH/LOW (for simple detection — object present or not) or a timed sequence of pulses encoding a measurement (the way the HC-SR04 uses pulse duration to represent distance, or the DHT11 uses pulse timing to send temperature and humidity as binary data). The advantage of digital output is noise immunity — a signal that's either 0V or 5V is much harder to corrupt with electrical noise than a signal that could be any voltage in between.

Then there are sensors that communicate over a protocol bus — I2C or SPI. These sensors do their own internal measurement, process it, and make the result available as a number over a two or four-wire bus. The MPU6050 IMU works this way. The OLED display also uses I2C, in the opposite direction (you send it display commands rather than it sending you sensor data).

6.2 Accuracy, Precision, Resolution — Not the Same Thing

These three words are used interchangeably in everyday speech but mean completely different things in engineering. Using the wrong one when reading a datasheet or describing a sensor will lead you to wrong conclusions.

Property What it means Example
Accuracy How close to the true value the reading is DHT11 accuracy: ±2°C. If actual temp is 25°C, it might read anywhere from 23°C to 27°C.
Precision How consistent the readings are with each other A sensor that always reads 27.3°C in a 25°C room is precise but not accurate. It's consistently wrong.
Resolution The smallest change the sensor can detect DHT11 resolution: 1°C. It can't distinguish between 25.3°C and 25.7°C — both would read as 25°C or 26°C.

For robotics, the sensor property that matters most depends on what you're using it for. For obstacle avoidance (is something close or not), accuracy in centimetres doesn't matter much — you just need consistent readings that change predictably with distance. For a temperature-controlled incubator, accuracy matters enormously. For counting encoder pulses to measure distance travelled, resolution matters most.

Always read the datasheet specification for the property that matters for your specific use case — not just the headline number on the product listing.

6.3 Sensor Noise and How to Handle It

Every sensor produces some noise — random variation in its output that doesn't correspond to a real change in the measured quantity. An ultrasonic sensor pointed at a stationary wall won't give you the same reading every time. It'll give you 34cm, then 33cm, then 35cm, then 34cm. That's noise. The wall didn't move.

Noise comes from multiple sources: electrical interference from motors and other components, thermal fluctuations inside the sensor itself, quantisation error in the ADC, and physical effects (air turbulence affecting ultrasonic readings, for example). You can't eliminate it entirely. You can reduce it.

The three main filtering techniques used in beginner robotics are:

  • Moving average: Keep the last N readings in an array. Return their average. Smooth and simple. The trade-off is lag — the filtered value responds to real changes slightly slower than the raw reading. Larger N = smoother but slower.
  • Median filter: Take N readings, sort them, return the middle value. Excellent at rejecting outliers (sudden spikes). Better than moving average for sensors prone to occasional wild readings.
  • Hysteresis: Not a filter exactly, but a decision-making technique. Instead of one threshold, use two — a high threshold to trigger ON and a lower threshold to trigger OFF. Prevents the robot from rapidly switching behaviour when a reading hovers near a single threshold.
// Moving average filter — 5 samples const int NUM_SAMPLES = 5; long readings[NUM_SAMPLES]; int readIndex = 0; long total = 0; long filteredDistance() { total -= readings[readIndex]; readings[readIndex] = getRawDistance(); total += readings[readIndex]; readIndex = (readIndex + 1) % NUM_SAMPLES; return total / NUM_SAMPLES; } // Hysteresis — two thresholds instead of one const int THRESHOLD_ON = 18; // Trigger stop when closer than 18cm const int THRESHOLD_OFF = 22; // Resume when farther than 22cm bool isStopped = false; void checkObstacle(long dist) { if (!isStopped && dist < THRESHOLD_ON) { stopMotors(); isStopped = true; } else if (isStopped && dist > THRESHOLD_OFF) { moveForward(200); isStopped = false; } }

6.4 Calibration: Making Readings Mean Something

Raw sensor readings are often just numbers. A soil moisture sensor returning 623 doesn't tell you whether the soil is wet or dry — it depends on the specific sensor, the specific soil type, and how it's been wired. Calibration is the process of connecting those raw numbers to real-world values you can reason about.

The simplest calibration method is two-point calibration: measure the sensor's output at two known reference conditions, then use those two points to define a linear mapping from raw values to real-world values.

// Two-point calibration for soil moisture sensor // Step 1: measure raw value in completely dry soil → dryValue // Step 2: measure raw value in completely wet soil → wetValue const int DRY_VALUE = 850; // Your sensor's dry reading (measure this) const int WET_VALUE = 380; // Your sensor's wet reading (measure this) int getMoisturePercent() { int raw = analogRead(MOISTURE_PIN); // Map: DRY_VALUE → 0%, WET_VALUE → 100% int percent = map(raw, DRY_VALUE, WET_VALUE, 0, 100); // Clamp to 0-100 in case readings go outside calibration range percent = constrain(percent, 0, 100); return percent; }

Arduino's built-in map() function handles the linear scaling. constrain() clamps the result to a valid range. After calibration, instead of reasoning about whether 623 means wet or dry, you get a percentage that means something immediately: 73% moisture. Act on that instead.

Important: calibration values are specific to your sensor and your environment. The DRY_VALUE and WET_VALUE in the example above will be different for every sensor unit and every soil type. You have to measure them yourself. Write them down. If you replace the sensor, recalibrate.

HC-SR04 — Ultrasonic Distance
Digital · Time-of-flight · Range: 2–400cm

The HC-SR04 works by time-of-flight measurement. It fires a burst of eight 40kHz ultrasonic pulses (above human hearing range) from the transmitter, then listens on the receiver for the echo. The time between transmission and reception, divided by the speed of sound (~343 m/s at 20°C), gives the distance. The Arduino doesn't need to know the speed of sound explicitly — dividing the pulse duration in microseconds by 58 gives centimetres directly (this comes from the round-trip calculation already built into the constant).

What affects its readings: temperature changes the speed of sound (about 0.6 m/s per °C), so the distance calculation becomes slightly less accurate in extreme temperatures. Soft or angled surfaces absorb or scatter the ultrasonic pulse, reducing or eliminating the echo. Objects smaller than the beam width (~15° cone) may not reflect cleanly. Readings under 2cm are unreliable because the sensor can't distinguish the transmitted pulse from the received echo at such short distances.

A practical issue in rover builds: when both motors are running, the electrical noise they generate can cause occasional wild readings (the echo pin picks up interference). Adding a 100µF capacitor across the motor power supply and keeping sensor wires physically away from motor wires reduces this significantly.

TCRT5000 — IR Line/Proximity
Digital/Analog · Reflectance · Range: 0–25mm

The TCRT5000 contains an IR LED and a phototransistor in the same package, aimed at the same surface. The IR LED shines downward (or forward), and the phototransistor measures how much IR light bounces back. Dark surfaces absorb IR; light surfaces reflect it. This difference in reflectance is what the sensor detects.

On a module board, there's usually a potentiometer (a small adjustable resistor, turned with a screwdriver) that sets the detection sensitivity threshold. The module outputs a digital signal (D0) — HIGH when no line detected, LOW when a line is detected — as well as an analog signal (A0) that gives the raw reflectance value for more nuanced processing.

For line-following, multiple TCRT5000 sensors are placed in a row under the robot, spaced across the width of the line being followed. The robot reads all sensors simultaneously and steers toward the sensors detecting the line. A three-sensor array (left, centre, right) is the minimum useful configuration. Five or seven sensors give smoother tracking.

Ambient light is a significant issue for IR sensors. Strong sunlight contains IR, which can overwhelm the sensor and cause false readings. Shield the sensor from direct sunlight or use a frequency-modulated IR emitter (which pulses at a specific frequency so the receiver can filter out constant-intensity ambient IR).

MPU6050 — IMU (Accelerometer + Gyroscope)
I2C · 6-DOF · Motion Sensing

The MPU6050 is the most complex sensor in this course, and understanding it properly requires separating what the accelerometer measures from what the gyroscope measures, and why you often need both.

The accelerometer measures acceleration forces — including gravity. When stationary, it detects only gravity (9.8 m/s² downward). By measuring which direction gravity is pulling in the sensor's reference frame, you can calculate tilt. If the sensor is perfectly level, gravity registers only on the Z axis. Tilt it 45°, and gravity has components on both Z and X (or Y). The math that extracts angle from these components is called tilt sensing or pitch/roll calculation.

The problem with accelerometers alone: any movement (vibration, a bump, acceleration from the robot moving) adds to the gravity vector and corrupts the tilt reading. A robot accelerating forward looks tilted forward to the accelerometer even if it's perfectly flat.

The gyroscope measures angular velocity — how fast the sensor is rotating. Integrating that rate over time gives you angle. If the gyroscope says you're rotating at 45°/s and 1 second passes, you've rotated 45°. Problem: gyroscopes drift. The integrated angle slowly wanders away from the true value over time due to tiny measurement errors accumulating.

The solution is sensor fusion — specifically a complementary filter or Kalman filter that combines accelerometer tilt data (accurate long-term, noisy short-term) with gyroscope rotation data (accurate short-term, drifts long-term). The result is stable, accurate orientation measurement. This is what the MPU6050's onboard DMP (Digital Motion Processor) does automatically if you use the MPU6050 DMP library.

Capacitive Soil Moisture Sensor
Analog · Capacitance · Agricultural

The capacitive soil moisture sensor works by measuring the dielectric constant of the soil — essentially how well the soil stores an electric field. Water has a much higher dielectric constant than air or dry soil, so wetter soil stores more charge, which the sensor reads as a higher capacitance. This gets converted to an output voltage: dry soil → higher voltage, wet soil → lower voltage (the exact relationship depends on the sensor circuit).

Unlike resistive sensors (which pass current through the soil and corrode), capacitive sensors don't make direct electrical contact with the soil through the sensing element. This makes them significantly more durable for long-term use — days to months in the ground without degradation.

The critical insight for using these correctly: raw values are meaningless without calibration. The range of readings you get depends on your specific sensor, your specific soil type, and the soil's compaction. Always calibrate by recording the dry reading (sensor in completely dry soil or air) and the wet reading (sensor in saturated soil or submerged in water), then use those as your map endpoints.

For agricultural automation (the irrigation control system from Module 01's final challenge), the typical implementation is: read moisture percentage, if below threshold turn on pump for N seconds, wait M minutes, read again. The waiting period prevents overwatering — soil moisture doesn't equalise instantly after watering.

DHT22 — Temperature & Humidity
Single-wire Digital · ±0.5°C accuracy

The DHT22 measures temperature using a thermistor (a resistor whose resistance changes predictably with temperature) and humidity using a capacitive humidity sensor (a capacitor whose capacitance changes with moisture in the air). Both measurements are processed internally by a small chip that communicates the result digitally over a single data wire.

The communication protocol is a proprietary single-wire serial protocol. The Arduino sends a start signal, the DHT22 responds with 40 bits of data encoding the humidity (16 bits), temperature (16 bits), and a checksum (8 bits for error detection). The DHT library handles all of this automatically — without the library, implementing this timing manually is fiddly and error-prone.

A critical limitation: the DHT22 needs at least 2 seconds between readings. Reading it faster than this returns stale data or errors. Always check the return value for NaN (not a number) — the sensor occasionally fails to respond cleanly, especially when first powered on. If you get a NaN, discard the reading and try again on the next cycle.

The DHT22 outperforms the DHT11 significantly in accuracy (±0.5°C vs ±2°C) and range (-40°C to +80°C vs 0°C to 50°C). For any project where temperature measurements need to be trusted, use the DHT22. The price difference is small.

LDR — Light Dependent Resistor
Analog · Photoresistance · Ambient Light

The LDR is one of the simplest sensors you'll work with. A semiconductor material (usually cadmium sulfide, CdS) whose resistance drops dramatically when light hits it. In complete darkness, resistance might be 1MΩ. In bright light, it might drop to 1kΩ. This resistance change is large enough to produce a very readable voltage change in a voltage divider circuit.

The voltage divider configuration: one end of the LDR connects to 5V, the other connects to both an analog input pin and one end of a 10kΩ fixed resistor. The other end of the fixed resistor goes to GND. As the LDR's resistance changes with light level, the voltage at the middle junction (the analog pin) changes proportionally. In bright light, LDR resistance is low — most of the voltage drops across the fixed resistor, so the pin reads high. In darkness, LDR resistance is high — most voltage drops across the LDR, so the pin reads low. (This is reversed if you swap LDR and resistor positions.)

LDRs respond slowly (milliseconds, not microseconds) — fine for detecting whether a room is lit or dark, not useful for detecting a fast-moving light source. They're also not spectrally calibrated — they respond differently to different colours of light, with peak sensitivity around green-yellow. They do not accurately measure lux (the scientific unit of illuminance) without additional calibration.

For light-following robots (two LDRs angled slightly outward, the robot steers toward the brighter side), the key is matching your two LDRs — using sensors from the same batch with similar characteristics. Mismatched LDRs will cause the robot to drift toward one side even in uniform light.

6.6 Sensor Fusion: When One Sensor Isn't Enough

Sometimes a single sensor can't give you a reliable picture of what the robot needs to know. Sensor fusion combines readings from multiple sensors to produce something better than any individual sensor could provide.

You've already seen one example: the MPU6050 combining accelerometer and gyroscope data for stable orientation. Here are two more practical examples for rover builds.

Example: Ultrasonic + IR for Obstacle Detection

The HC-SR04 is accurate at medium distances (5–200cm) but has a blind zone under 2cm and struggles with soft surfaces. An IR proximity sensor works at very short range (0–25cm) but gives noisy, distance-uncalibrated output.

Combining both: use the HC-SR04 for distance measurement and approach behaviour, switch to IR confirmation when the robot is very close (under 10cm) to verify the obstacle is actually there. If the HC-SR04 says 3cm but the IR sensor sees nothing, the ultrasonic probably got a false reading — keep moving. If both agree, definitely stop.

Example: Soil Moisture + Temperature for Irrigation Logic

A soil moisture sensor alone might trigger irrigation even when it's not needed — moisture readings change with temperature (hot soil reads dryer than it actually is). Adding a temperature sensor lets you apply a temperature correction factor: if temperature is above 35°C, the moisture threshold that triggers watering is adjusted upward to compensate for the sensor's thermal behaviour.

This is a simple form of context-aware sensing — the robot interprets one sensor's reading in light of another sensor's reading. More complex versions of this idea run inside every modern weather station, self-driving vehicle, and industrial process control system.

6.7 Building a Sensor Test Rig

Before you integrate a sensor into a full robot build, always test it standalone. A sensor test rig is a minimal circuit — just the sensor, an Arduino, and the Serial monitor — that lets you characterise the sensor's behaviour in your specific environment before you have to worry about motors, motor noise, power supply variations, and mechanical issues complicating the picture.

What to document during a sensor test:

  • The baseline reading — what does the sensor report in your typical operating environment with nothing interesting happening?
  • The noise range — how much does the reading vary when nothing is changing? This sets your minimum useful threshold difference.
  • The response at extremes — what does it read at the minimum and maximum of what you want to detect? Are those readings distinct enough to act on reliably?
  • The response to interference — what happens when you run a motor near it? When sunlight hits it directly? When temperature changes?
  • The recovery behaviour — if you remove a stimulus abruptly, how quickly does the reading return to baseline?

Twenty minutes with a sensor and the Serial monitor before integrating it into a build saves hours of debugging tangled hardware and software issues later. This discipline, applied consistently, is one of the clearest markers of engineering experience.

Sensors convert physical reality into electrical signals

No electronics yet. Get the physical structure right first.

The chassis is the physical platform everything mounts on. It doesn't need to be beautiful. It needs to be rigid enough that components don't shift when the robot moves, large enough to hold everything, and light enough that the motors can drive it without straining.

A piece of cardboard doubled over and glued together (about 15cm × 20cm) is a perfectly functional chassis for this build. Foam board from a stationery shop is better — stiffer and lighter. Thin plywood works well if you have it. A plastic food container lid can work. Bought acrylic chassis plates are the most professional option but are entirely unnecessary for a working rover.

Step 1 Mount the Motors

The two drive motors mount on opposite sides of the chassis, facing outward, so their shafts (and wheels) stick out to the left and right. They should be at the same height and the same distance from the front of the chassis, otherwise the robot will pull to one side.

Secure them with cable ties (zip ties), hot glue, or small bolts through the motor mounting holes (most TT motors have two mounting holes on the flat face). Hot glue is fastest. Cable ties through slots cut in the chassis are the most secure without tools.

The motors should be positioned roughly one-third of the way from the rear of the chassis. This leaves room at the front for the sensor and at the back for the battery, which should be as far back as possible to keep weight over the drive wheels.

Step 2 Add the Front Caster

A two-wheeled robot with drive wheels at the rear (or centre) needs a third contact point at the front to stay level. This is a caster — a freely rotating wheel or ball that supports the front without adding resistance to turning.

The simplest option: a marble or ball bearing glued into a small cup made from bottle cap or foam, mounted at the front underside of the chassis. A bought ball caster (a small plastic housing with a ball bearing that rolls in any direction) is better if you can find one. A third wheel from a salvaged toy also works — mount it so it can rotate freely on its axle.

The height of the caster matters. When all three contact points are on a flat surface, the chassis should be level — not tilted forward or backward. Adjust caster height with layers of foam or cardboard underneath it until the chassis sits flat.

Step 3 Mount the Electronics Positions

Before gluing anything down, do a dry layout — place all the electronics on the chassis without attaching them and check that everything fits with room for wiring. A typical layout for this build:

Front: HC-SR04 sensor, facing forward. Mount it at the front edge of the chassis, elevated slightly so it has a clear view forward without the chassis edge entering its beam.

Centre: Arduino Uno and breadboard, side by side. The Arduino USB port should face a direction where you can reach it with a cable for reprogramming without disassembling the robot.

Rear: L298N motor driver and battery. The L298N generates heat — keep it away from the Arduino and sensor. The battery is the heaviest component — positioning it directly over or behind the drive wheels keeps the robot stable.

Once you're happy with the layout, secure each component. Velcro (hook-and-loop tape) is excellent — it holds firmly but allows removal. Hot glue works but is permanent. Double-sided foam tape is a decent middle ground.

  • Wheels are attached to both motor shafts
  • Front caster is mounted and chassis sits level on a flat surface
  • Robot can roll smoothly forward when pushed by hand without wobbling or binding

7.3 Phase 2 — Wiring

02
Electrical Connections
No power connected yet. Wire everything first, verify, then connect power.

Wire the entire circuit before connecting any power. This is the single most important habit in electronics. A wiring mistake with power connected can destroy a component in milliseconds. A wiring mistake discovered before power is applied costs you nothing but time.

Work through the wiring sections below in order. After each section, trace every connection with your finger against the diagram before moving to the next.

Section A — Motors to L298N
Left motor: both wires L298N terminals OUT1 and OUT2. Either orientation — you can correct direction in code if it goes backwards.
Right motor: both wires L298N terminals OUT3 and OUT4. Either orientation initially.
100µF capacitor Positive leg to OUT1/OUT3 side of L298N power terminal. Negative leg to GND. Smooths motor voltage spikes. Observe polarity — longer leg is positive.
Section B — Power Supply
Battery positive (+) L298N 12V terminal (it accepts any voltage from 5V–35V — the label just says 12V historically). This powers the motors.
Battery negative (−) L298N GND terminal AND Arduino GND pin. Both must connect to the same battery negative. This is the common ground.
L298N 5V output pin Arduino 5V pin — only if your battery is 7V or above and your L298N module has this output pin available. Check your specific module. If unsure, power the Arduino separately via USB or its own barrel jack.
Do not connect the battery yet. Complete all wiring first. Specifically: ensure the L298N GND and Arduino GND are connected together before any power is applied. A missing common ground is the most common reason this circuit doesn't work, and it's also one of the hardest to diagnose after the fact.
Section C — L298N Control Pins to Arduino
L298N: IN1 Arduino pin 8 (digital)
L298N: IN2 Arduino pin 9 (digital)
L298N: ENA Arduino pin 10 (PWM — marked ~10). Remove the jumper that may be bridging ENA — if it's jumpered, the motor always runs at full speed and PWM control does nothing.
L298N: IN3 Arduino pin 7 (digital)
L298N: IN4 Arduino pin 6 (digital)
L298N: ENB Arduino pin 5 (PWM — marked ~5). Remove ENB jumper same as ENA.
Section D — HC-SR04 to Arduino (via breadboard)
HC-SR04: VCC Arduino 5V pin (or breadboard 5V rail connected to Arduino 5V)
HC-SR04: GND Arduino GND (or breadboard GND rail)
HC-SR04: Trig Arduino pin 12 (digital output)
HC-SR04: Echo Arduino pin 11 (digital input)
Section E — Status LED
330Ω resistor: one leg Arduino pin 13 (digital output)
330Ω resistor: other leg LED anode (longer leg)
LED cathode (shorter leg) Arduino GND (or breadboard GND rail)

7.4 Phase 3 — The Code

03
Software
Upload and test before the robot moves. Verify each layer separately.

The code is built in layers. Upload and test each layer before adding the next. This way, when something doesn't work, you know exactly which layer introduced the problem.

Layer 1 Sensor Test — Verify the HC-SR04 is Working

Before connecting the battery to the motors, power the Arduino via USB only. Upload this sketch and open the Serial monitor at 9600 baud. Wave your hand in front of the sensor. The distance reading should change as you move your hand closer and further away. If it reads 0 constantly or shows wildly inconsistent values, there's a wiring problem on the sensor — check Trig, Echo, VCC, and GND connections.

// LAYER 1: Sensor test — upload this first const int TRIG = 12; const int ECHO = 11; const int LED = 13; void setup() { pinMode(TRIG, OUTPUT); pinMode(ECHO, INPUT); pinMode(LED, OUTPUT); Serial.begin(9600); Serial.println("Sensor test started"); } long getDistance() { digitalWrite(TRIG, LOW); delayMicroseconds(2); digitalWrite(TRIG, HIGH); delayMicroseconds(10); digitalWrite(TRIG, LOW); return pulseIn(ECHO, HIGH) / 58; } void loop() { long dist = getDistance(); Serial.print("Distance: "); Serial.print(dist); Serial.println(" cm"); if (dist > 0 && dist < 20) { digitalWrite(LED, HIGH); // LED on when obstacle close } else { digitalWrite(LED, LOW); } delay(100); }

Leave this running for two minutes. Check that the LED lights reliably when your hand is within 20cm and turns off when you move away. Check the Serial monitor for consistent, reasonable readings. Note the noise range — if readings jump by more than 3–4cm with nothing changing, note that and we'll add the median filter in the full code.

Layer 2 Motor Test — Verify Both Motors Work Correctly

Now connect the battery. Upload this motor test sketch. It runs each motor in sequence: left forward, left reverse, right forward, right reverse, both forward, both reverse, stop. Watch each motor carefully. If a motor runs backwards when it should go forward, swap the two wires going to that motor's L298N output terminals (OUT1/OUT2 or OUT3/OUT4). Note which direction corresponds to forward for each motor — you may need to swap wires or invert the IN1/IN2 logic in the final code.

// LAYER 2: Motor test — verify direction and speed const int IN1=8, IN2=9, ENA=10; // Left motor const int IN3=7, IN4=6, ENB=5; // Right motor void setup() { pinMode(IN1,OUTPUT); pinMode(IN2,OUTPUT); pinMode(ENA,OUTPUT); pinMode(IN3,OUTPUT); pinMode(IN4,OUTPUT); pinMode(ENB,OUTPUT); Serial.begin(9600); } void loop() { Serial.println("Left forward"); digitalWrite(IN1,HIGH); digitalWrite(IN2,LOW); analogWrite(ENA, 180); analogWrite(ENB, 0); delay(1500); Serial.println("Right forward"); digitalWrite(IN3,HIGH); digitalWrite(IN4,LOW); analogWrite(ENA, 0); analogWrite(ENB, 180); delay(1500); Serial.println("Both forward"); digitalWrite(IN1,HIGH); digitalWrite(IN2,LOW); digitalWrite(IN3,HIGH); digitalWrite(IN4,LOW); analogWrite(ENA, 180); analogWrite(ENB, 180); delay(2000); Serial.println("Stop"); analogWrite(ENA, 0); analogWrite(ENB, 0); delay(2000); }

During the "both forward" phase, place the rover on the floor and watch it go. Does it drive roughly straight? A slight drift is normal due to motor manufacturing variation — you can compensate by reducing the PWM value of the faster side slightly. Does it drive at a reasonable speed? If it barely moves, your motors may need a higher PWM value (motors have a dead band — try increasing from 180 to 200 or 220).

7.5 Phase 4 — First Run and Tuning

04
Test and Tune
It works. Now make it work well.

Place the rover on a clear floor with obstacles around it (chairs, walls, boxes). Press reset or disconnect and reconnect power. Watch what happens. Open the Serial monitor if you have USB access.

The first run almost certainly won't be perfect. That's not a failure — it's the start of tuning. Here's what to adjust and how:

Behaviour Likely cause Fix
Drives straight but veers left or right over distance One motor is slightly faster than the other — manufacturing variation Reduce SPEED on the faster side. Add a SPEED_LEFT and SPEED_RIGHT constant and tune them separately until it tracks straight.
Stops too far from obstacles STOP_DIST threshold too high Reduce STOP_DIST constant. Test at 15cm then 10cm until you find a distance that feels right for the environment.
Doesn't stop before hitting obstacles STOP_DIST too low, or sensor readings are noisy and missing the obstacle Increase STOP_DIST. Add more samples to the median filter (take 5 readings instead of 3).
Turns the same direction every time and gets stuck in corners Always turning right — expected for the basic code Add randomised turn direction: use random(0,2) to pick left or right on each obstacle event.
Rover stutters or twitches without obstacles present Sensor noise causing false detections near the threshold Increase STOP_DIST and RESUME_DIST gap (hysteresis). Ensure 100µF capacitor is across motor power supply.
Motors don't move but sensor works ENA/ENB jumpers still in place, or common ground missing Remove ENA and ENB jumpers from L298N. Verify GND connections.
One motor doesn't move Wiring fault on that motor's channel Verify IN3/IN4/ENB connections and OUT3/OUT4 motor terminal connections.

7.6 Troubleshooting Guide

When things go wrong (and they will), work systematically. Changing two things at once when debugging means you don't know which change fixed the problem. Change one thing, test, observe, then change the next.

Nothing happens when power is applied

Check 1: Is the Arduino power LED on? If not, the Arduino isn't receiving power. Check the battery connection and the barrel jack or Vin pin.

Check 2: Is code uploaded? The Arduino needs code in its memory. Upload Layer 1 first via USB.

Check 3: Is the Serial monitor showing anything? Open it and press reset. If you see nothing, the baud rate may be mismatched — ensure it's set to 9600.

Sensor always reads 0 or always reads 400

Always 0: pulseIn() is timing out (no echo received). Check Echo pin connection, check that VCC is 5V not 3.3V, check that nothing is blocking the sensor within 2cm.

Always 400: In the full rover code, 400 is the timeout substitute value (set deliberately). If you see 400 constantly in Layer 1 code, the sensor isn't receiving an echo — same checks as above.

Motors spin in wrong direction

Swap the two wires going to that motor's L298N output terminals. For the left motor, swap OUT1 and OUT2. For the right motor, swap OUT3 and OUT4. Alternatively, swap HIGH and LOW in the IN1/IN2 or IN3/IN4 lines in code.

L298N gets very hot quickly

The L298N drops about 2V across its output stage and dissipates that as heat. Normal operation produces some warmth. Very hot (can't touch it after 30 seconds) usually means a motor is stalling or the motor voltage is too high for the motors you're using. Check that motors can spin freely when disconnected from the chassis. Ensure the L298N heatsink is making good contact with the chip — sometimes the thermal pad needs reseating.

Rover drives in circles instead of straight

Motor speed mismatch — one motor is faster. Add separate speed constants for left and right motors in the code and tune them independently. Start by reducing the faster motor's PWM value by 10, test, reduce by another 10 if still drifting, until straight.

Sensor readings go wild when motors run

Motor electrical noise coupling into the sensor or signal lines. Ensure 100µF capacitor is across motor power terminals. Keep sensor wires physically away from motor wires (route them on opposite sides of the chassis). Add a small delay (5ms) after stopping motors before taking a sensor reading if the issue persists.

7.7 Extensions — What to Add Next

The base rover works. Here are natural extensions to try, roughly in order of complexity. Each one exercises a concept from earlier modules in a new context.

  • Randomised turning. Instead of always turning right, use random(0,2) to pick left or right randomly each time an obstacle is encountered. The rover becomes less predictable and navigates more interesting environments.
  • Speed proportional to distance. Instead of full speed or stopped, make the rover slow down gradually as it approaches an obstacle. Map the distance (20–100cm) to a speed (0–255) using Arduino's map() function. The approach feels smoother and more controlled.
  • Add a second sensor. Mount a second HC-SR04 (or an IR sensor) facing to one side. When the rover is about to turn, check the side sensor first and turn away from whichever side has less space. The rover now makes smarter navigation decisions.
  • OLED display. Add the 0.96" OLED from Module 03. Display the current state and distance reading on screen. Now you can monitor the rover's behaviour without a Serial monitor connection — useful when running it untethered.
  • Line following mode. Add three TCRT5000 IR sensors under the front of the chassis. Write a second operating mode that follows a black line on a white surface. Add a push button that switches between obstacle avoidance mode and line following mode.
  • Tilt detection. Mount an MPU6050 on the chassis. If the rover tilts beyond a threshold (it's fallen off a surface or tipped over), stop the motors and sound a buzzer. Simple but satisfying.

The rover navigating around your floor autonomously — avoiding obstacles, adjusting course, making decisions without any human input — is running the exact same three-step loop you read about in Module 01. The principles inside that rover are the same principles inside every self-driving vehicle, every warehouse robot, every autonomous drone. The scale is different. The sensors are more sophisticated. 8.1 The Three Paths Forward

There are broadly three directions you can go from here, and they're not mutually exclusive. Most people who go deep in robotics end up touching all three over time.

⬡
Deeper Hardware
Electronics · PCB Design · Embedded Systems

This path goes further into how the hardware works — designing your own circuit boards, understanding communication protocols properly, working with more powerful microcontrollers, and eventually working at the intersection of hardware and software in ways that most programmers can't.

This is the path that leads to embedded systems engineering, hardware product development, and the kind of deep electronics knowledge that makes you genuinely rare. Most software engineers can write code. Very few can design the hardware the code runs on.

01 Complete this course's extensions from Module 07. Add line following, the OLED display, the MPU6050. Get comfortable modifying and extending code you didn't write from scratch.
02 Move to the ESP32. More powerful than the Arduino Uno, has built-in Wi-Fi and Bluetooth, runs at 240MHz, supports MicroPython as well as C++. The transition from Uno to ESP32 is a natural next step that opens up IoT and wireless robotics.
03 Learn KiCad (free PCB design software). Design a simple PCB that replaces your breadboard — just the components for one of your working circuits, laid out properly and sent to a manufacturer like JLCPCB ($2 for 5 boards). Holding a PCB you designed is a significant milestone.
04 Study PID control. Proportional-Integral-Derivative control is the algorithm that makes robots move smoothly and precisely — used in line followers, self-balancing robots, drone stabilisation, and industrial motion control. It builds directly on the motor control and sensor reading you already know.
05 Build a self-balancing robot. Two wheels, an MPU6050, PID control. Widely regarded as the canonical intermediate robotics project — it brings together everything from this course plus PID in one build.
◈
Deeper Software
ROS · Computer Vision · AI on Edge · SLAM

This path goes further into the intelligence layer — more sophisticated algorithms, computer vision, machine learning running directly on robots, and eventually the full software stack that powers autonomous systems. This is where robotics meets AI in a concrete, physical way.

If you're already comfortable in Python (which you are), this path has a shallower entry curve than the hardware path. The tools are mature, the community is large, and the problems are genuinely interesting.

01 Move to the Raspberry Pi. A full Linux computer the size of a credit card. Runs Python natively, has a camera interface, GPIO pins for hardware control, and is the standard platform for robotics projects that need real computing power. Connect it to your Arduino-controlled hardware for a split architecture — Raspberry Pi for high-level logic, Arduino for low-level hardware control.
02 Learn OpenCV. Open Computer Vision library. Free, runs in Python, works on Raspberry Pi. Lets your robot see — detect objects, track colours, read QR codes, recognise faces. A Raspberry Pi camera module costs very little. OpenCV is free. The combination is genuinely powerful.
03 Learn ROS 2 (Robot Operating System). The standard middleware for serious robotics development. Handles communication between robot components, simulation, visualisation, and provides ready-made implementations of common algorithms. Steep learning curve, but industry-standard — knowing ROS makes you employable in robotics.
04 Study SLAM (Simultaneous Localisation and Mapping). The algorithm family that lets a robot build a map of an unknown environment while simultaneously figuring out where it is within that map. This is what self-driving cars do. It's hard, but once you understand the sensor fusion and probability theory behind it, it clicks.
05 TensorFlow Lite on embedded hardware. You taught yourself TensorFlow. It runs on microcontrollers now — TensorFlow Lite for Microcontrollers lets you run trained ML models directly on hardware without a cloud connection. Gesture recognition, keyword spotting, anomaly detection — all possible on low-power embedded devices.
◉
Build Something Real
Applied Robotics · Problem-Driven · Impact

The third path doesn't go deeper into hardware or software specifically. It applies what you already know to actual problems — in agriculture, in healthcare access, in infrastructure monitoring, in education. This path is about building things that matter in the specific context you're in, using the skills you have, and learning what you need as you go.

This path is underrated. The robotics and embedded systems space produces a huge amount of technology built by people in rich countries for problems in rich countries. The engineering problems specific to sub-Saharan Africa (intermittent power, limited connectivity, extreme heat, dust, cost constraints, specific agricultural conditions) are genuinely different from the problems most robotics engineers are solving. That difference is an opportunity, not a disadvantage.

01 Pick one problem you care about. Not a technology — a problem. Unreliable water pumps. Crop disease detection. Grid monitoring. Postharvest grain storage conditions. Pick something specific and local that you have firsthand knowledge of.
02 Build a prototype with what you already know. The components and skills from this course are enough to build a first version of most sensor-based monitoring or simple automation systems. It won't be polished. Build it anyway. The act of building exposes the real problems.
03 Document everything. Photos, schematics, code, what broke, what you changed and why. Documentation is how a prototype becomes something other people can learn from, build on, or fund.
04 Share it. GitHub for code. YouTube for build videos. Hackster.io or Instructables for project writeups. The global robotics community actively looks for novel applications. A well-documented project solving a real African infrastructure or agricultural problem will get attention.
05 Enter competitions. First Global, iGEM, Technovation, Conrad Challenge, Regeneron ISEF. These exist specifically to support young engineers building things that matter. The rover you just built is already within the capability range of entry-level competition projects.

8.3 The Zero-Kit Constraint, Going Forward

Throughout this course, every concept has had a note on what you can salvage or substitute when standard components aren't available. That constraint is real — but it's worth reframing it.

The engineers who built the first computers were working with vacuum tubes in conditions that would be considered extreme resource constraints by modern standards. The teams that built the technologies behind the Apollo missions were debugging with logic gates and oscilloscopes, not IDEs and simulation software. Constraint is not a disadvantage exclusive to you. It's the default condition of engineering at the frontier of anything.

What the Zero-Kit Constraint actually gives you is something most well-resourced engineers don't develop early enough: the ability to understand your tools deeply because you can't rely on the tools doing the work for you. Someone who has soldered a circuit from salvaged components understands that circuit better than someone who ordered everything pre-assembled from a kit. The harder path, when it doesn't break you, builds real competence.

The constraint is not the obstacle. The constraint is part of the education.

As you go further, resources will become more available — through competitions, through university access, through online communities that sometimes run component donation programmes for students in low-resource environments. Hackster.io and Arduino run free hardware programmes. The Raspberry Pi Foundation has a community grants process. First Global connections sometimes include component access for team members.

In the meantime: salvage aggressively, document everything, and build with what you have. The ideas are the same regardless of the cost of the components.

8.4 A Note on the Gap Between Here and There

There's going to be a period — probably a long one — where you know enough to know how much you don't know, but not enough yet to build the things you can clearly imagine. That gap is uncomfortable. It's also universal. Every engineer you admire has been in it.

The only way through it is building things. Not reading about building things. Not watching videos about building things. Building things, having them not work, figuring out why, fixing them, and repeating that until they do work. The Serial monitor and the multimeter are your instruments. Debugging is the actual work. The tutorials are just pointing you at the next thing to try.

The rover you built doesn't navigate perfectly. The code has rough edges. The chassis is probably not beautiful. None of that is the point. The point is that you took components, applied concepts, wrote code, wired circuits, encountered problems, solved them, and produced something that moves autonomously because of decisions you made. That process — not the rover — is what you're actually developing.

The engineers who change things aren't the ones who waited until they felt ready. They're the ones who built before they were ready, broke things publicly, learned from it, and kept going.
